Important timescales are the chemical $t_c$, the Kolmogorov $t_k$, and the large (or integral) $t_t$.

This sets up the different flame regimes, as seen in the Peters diagram.

The y-axis is the ratio of the turbulent velocity fluctuation to the unstretched flame speed (consumption speed), $u_{rms}/s_L^0$.

    \label{fig:peters-diagram}

\paragraph{Wrinkled flamelets} have turbulent velocity fluctuations smaller than the flame speed, so the flame's movement is dominated by the average flow speed.

\paragraph{Corrugated flames} are influenced by the turbulent part of the flow, but $Ka < 1$. This means the Kolmogorov scale has a timescale larger than the chemical timescale, and therefore does not contribute to mixing.

\paragraph{Thin reaction zones} have $Ka > 1$, meaning the Kolmogorov scale influences mixing, but $Da > 1$, meaning the large scales do not influence mixing.

Independent of $Da$, for $Ka$ large enough (around 100 for methane), the Kolmogorov scale not only influences mixing but also mixes the flame after the radical separation, within the thin reaction zone.

\paragraph{Distributed reaction zones} occur for $Da < 1$. At this stage, even the large scales influence mixing. The timescale of the large scales is smaller than the chemical timescale.


{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Turbulence is phenomenologically described as a collection of eddies of different sizes through which kinetic energy \textbf{cascades} from large to small scales, being dissipated at the smallest scale~\cite{lecture8,pope2000turbulent}. The largest, energy-containing eddies have the \textbf{integral length scale} $l_t$ (of the order of the geometry, e.g.\ the jet diameter) and velocity fluctuation $u'(l_t) \approx u'_{rms}$, giving the \textbf{integral (large-eddy) time scale} $t_t = l_t / u'(l_t)$ --- the eddy turnover time. From the decay of a large eddy, the energy dissipation rate is estimated as $\varepsilon \approx u'^3(l_t)/l_t$ $[\mathrm{m^2/s^3}]$: the large scales \textbf{determine} $\varepsilon$, while the smallest scales \textbf{depend on} $\varepsilon$ and the viscosity $\nu$. Dimensional analysis (Kolmogorov hypothesis) then gives the \textbf{Kolmogorov scales} $\eta_k = (\nu^3/\varepsilon)^{1/4}$, $t_k = (\nu/\varepsilon)^{1/2}$, $v_k = (\nu \varepsilon)^{1/4}$, at which turbulent kinetic energy is dissipated into heat~\cite{pope2000turbulent}. The separation of scales is governed by the \textbf{turbulent Reynolds number} $\mathrm{Re}_t = u'(l_t)\, l_t / \nu$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:re-number}):
\begin{equation*}
\frac{l_t}{\eta_k} = \mathrm{Re}_t^{3/4}, \qquad \frac{t_t}{t_k} = \mathrm{Re}_t^{1/2}, \qquad \frac{v_t}{v_k} = \mathrm{Re}_t^{1/4},
\end{equation*}
so at high $\mathrm{Re}_t$ the flow contains a wide range ($l_t$ down to $\eta_k$, with the inertial subrange in between) of length and time scales that can interact with the flame.

The \textbf{chemical time scale} is estimated as the time a freely propagating flat laminar flame needs to advance over its own thickness, $t_{chem} = \delta / s_L^0$, with $\delta = \alpha / s_L^0$ the diffusive flame thickness and $s_L^0$ the unstretched laminar burning velocity. Comparing it with the two turbulent times defines the two key dimensionless groups: the \textbf{Damk\"ohler number} $\mathrm{Da} = t_t / t_{chem} = (l_t\, s_L^0)/(u'_{rms}\, \delta)$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:damkohler-number}), comparing chemistry with the \textit{large} eddies, and the \textbf{Karlovitz number} $\mathrm{Ka} = t_{chem}/t_k = (u'_{rms}/s_L^0)^{3/2} (l_t/\delta)^{-1/2}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:karlovitz-number}), comparing it with the \textit{smallest} eddies~\cite{lecture8,peters2000turbulent}. They are not independent: $\mathrm{Da}^2 \mathrm{Ka}^2 = (t_t/t_k)^2 = \mathrm{Re}_t^2$. Physically, $\mathrm{Da} = 1$ marks the point where even the large eddies turn over faster than the flame burns ($t_t < t_{chem}$ for $\mathrm{Da}<1$: \textit{all} eddies can disturb the front while it passes), while $\mathrm{Ka} = 1$ marks where the smallest eddies become faster than the chemistry ($t_k < t_{chem}$ for $\mathrm{Ka}>1$) and small enough to enter the flame structure.

These ratios organise the \textbf{premixed turbulent combustion regimes} in the Borghi--Peters diagram (Fig.~\ref{fig:peters-diagram})~\cite{peters2000turbulent,warnatz2006combustion,roekaerts2010jmbc}, with axes $u'_{rms}/s_L^0$ (velocity ratio) and $l_t/\delta$ (length ratio), on which the lines $\mathrm{Re}_t = 1$, $\mathrm{Ka} = 1$, $\mathrm{Ka} = 100$ and $\mathrm{Da} = 1$ appear as straight lines in log-log coordinates. Below $\mathrm{Re}_t = 1$ the flow is laminar. In the \textbf{wrinkled flamelet} regime ($u'_{rms} < s_L^0$, $\mathrm{Ka} < 1$) turbulence only mildly wrinkles a flame that burns faster than even the largest eddies turn over. In the \textbf{corrugated flamelet} regime ($u'_{rms} > s_L^0$, still $\mathrm{Ka} < 1$) the large eddies strongly convolute the front and can form pockets, but even the Kolmogorov eddies remain slower and larger than the flame, so the internal flame structure stays that of a stretched laminar flame (flamelet). Crossing $\mathrm{Ka} = 1$ into the \textbf{thin reaction zones} regime ($1 < \mathrm{Ka} < 100$), the smallest eddies are now smaller than the preheat zone and penetrate it, enhancing mixing of heat and intermediate species there; however, they are still larger/slower than the \textbf{inner reaction zone} (thickness $\delta_r \approx \delta/10$, the layer of intense heat release, steep temperature gradient and short-lived radicals), which remains intact. Beyond $\mathrm{Ka} \approx 100$ (for methane/air; equivalently $\mathrm{Ka}_\delta = \mathrm{Ka}\,(\delta_r/\delta)^2 \approx 1$), in the \textbf{broken/distributed reaction zones} regime, Kolmogorov eddies disrupt even the inner reaction layer, mixing radicals away faster than chemistry can sustain the front, which can lead to local extinction; finally, for $\mathrm{Da} < 1$ (\textbf{well-stirred reactor} regime) all turbulent scales, including the integral ones, mix faster than the chemistry, reaction becomes distributed in space and the mixture behaves as a homogeneous reactor with no identifiable flame front. The regime location thus dictates which modelling strategy (flamelet-based vs.\ reactor/PDF-based) is appropriate~\cite{veynante2002turbulent}, and OH-imaging experiments confirm this morphology evolution from smooth wrinkled fronts to broken, distributed reaction zones as $u'_{rms}/s_L^0$ increases~\cite{ciccarelli2008flame}.
}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The phrase ``computation of turbulent flames'' refers to how the turbulent spectrum is treated, and there are \textbf{three main approaches}, distinguished by how much of the turbulent energy spectrum is \emph{resolved} versus \emph{modelled}~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,poinsot2012theoretical}: \textbf{RANS} (Reynolds/Favre-Averaged Navier--Stokes), \textbf{LES} (Large-Eddy Simulation) and \textbf{DNS} (Direct Numerical Simulation). In RANS the equations are time/ensemble averaged, so \emph{none} of the turbulent fluctuations are resolved and the \emph{entire} spectrum --- from the integral scale down to Kolmogorov --- is modelled; only the mean field is computed. In LES a spatial low-pass filter of width $\Delta$ is applied: the large, energy-containing, geometry-dependent eddies are computed directly and only the (more universal) subgrid scales smaller than $\Delta$ are modelled. In DNS no turbulence model is used at all: the instantaneous Navier--Stokes and species equations are solved on a grid fine enough to resolve the Kolmogorov scale $\eta_k$ and a time step resolving $t_k$, capturing every scale of motion and the full flame structure.

Their \textbf{advantages and drawbacks} follow directly from this. \textbf{RANS} is by far the cheapest (steady simulations, coarse grids) and remains the workhorse for industrial gas-turbine design; its drawbacks are that the turbulence model (typically $k$--$\varepsilon$) introduces large modelling errors, it cannot capture inherently unsteady phenomena (thermo-acoustic instabilities, large-scale mixing, intermittency, local extinction/re-ignition), and the \emph{whole} of the turbulence--chemistry interaction must be modelled. \textbf{LES} resolves precisely the large eddies that govern turbulent mixing and unsteady dynamics, giving much better predictions of mixing-controlled and unsteady combustion at a cost intermediate between RANS and DNS; its drawbacks are that it is still too expensive for routine design iterations, requires high-quality unsteady boundary/inflow conditions, and --- crucially for combustion --- the flame and reaction zone are almost always \emph{thinner than the filter width} $\Delta$, so chemistry lives entirely at the subgrid level and still needs a model (see the next question). \textbf{DNS} is the most accurate, model-free, and the reference (``numerical experiment'') against which RANS/LES closures are validated; its decisive drawback is cost --- the number of grid points scales as $\mathrm{Re}_t^{9/4}$, and the stiff multi-species chemistry adds further expense --- which restricts it to low-to-moderate Reynolds numbers, simple geometries and small domains, well below real engine conditions~\cite{pope2000turbulent,veynante2002turbulent}.

The reason RANS and LES need a dedicated \textbf{combustion (turbulence--chemistry interaction) model} is the central difficulty of the field. The Favre-averaged/filtered species equation
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \bar\rho\, \tilde Y_k}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\!\left(\bar\rho\, \tilde u_i\, \tilde Y_k\right) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\!\left[\left(D_{k,\mathrm{mix}} + D_T\right)\frac{\partial \tilde Y_k}{\partial x_i}\right] + \overline{\dot\omega}_k
\end{equation*}
contains the \textbf{mean reaction rate} $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$, which because of the strongly non-linear Arrhenius law is \emph{not} a function of the mean quantities: expanding $\dot w = \beta(\bar\phi_A+\phi_A')(\bar\phi_B+\phi_B')\exp\!\big(-T_{act}/(\bar T+T')\big)$ shows it depends on all the unknown moments of the fluctuations, so $\overline{\dot\omega}_k \neq \dot\omega_k(\tilde Y,\tilde T)$~\cite{lecture10,warnatz2006combustion}. (The equation is Favre-weighted precisely to avoid unclosed density-fluctuation terms.) The various combustion models are different strategies to close $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$. \textbf{General approaches:} the \textbf{eddy break-up}/eddy-dissipation model assumes ``mixed $=$ burnt'' (chemistry infinitely fast compared with mixing) and ties the rate to the turbulent mixing time $k/\varepsilon$, e.g.\ $\overline{\dot\omega}_k = -C_{EBU}\,\bar\rho\,(\varepsilon/k)\,\bar Y_k(1-\bar Y_k)$ --- cheap and robust but crude (no finite-rate chemistry, no extinction, no pollutants)~\cite{lecture10}; \textbf{transported-PDF/FDF} methods are the most general (treated below). \textbf{Premixed approaches:} \textbf{flame surface density} models treat the flame as a thin interface separating reactants from products and solve a transport equation for the flame area per unit volume, which sets the propagation rate; \textbf{flamelet/progress-variable} methods (with $c$ running from $0$ unburnt to $1$ burnt), and the equivalent \emph{flame-speed-closure} formulation, exploit the flamelet assumption that turbulence does not alter the inner flame structure. \textbf{Non-premixed approaches:} \textbf{mixture-fraction} flamelet models (composition tabulated against $\xi$, parametrised further by scalar dissipation rate or strain), and \textbf{Conditional Moment Closure} (transport equations for species conditioned on $\xi$). For \emph{partially premixed} systems, \textbf{Flamelet-Generated Manifolds (FGM)} tabulate the thermochemistry against \emph{both} mixture fraction $\xi$ and progress variable $c$, $\tilde Y_k = \int\!\!\int Y_k^{fgm}(\xi,c)\,\tilde P(\xi,c)\,d\xi\,dc$, fusing the premixed and non-premixed flamelet ideas~\cite{lecture10,vanoijen2000fgm}.

\textbf{Transported PDF --- the exact transport equation.} In transported-PDF (RANS) or filtered-density-function (LES) methods the mean rate is obtained from $\overline{\dot\omega}_k(\mathbf{x},t) = \int \dot\omega_k(\boldsymbol\psi)\,\tilde P(\boldsymbol\psi;\mathbf{x},t)\,d\boldsymbol\psi$, where $\tilde P(\boldsymbol\psi;\mathbf{x},t)$ is the joint PDF of the thermochemical state vector $\boldsymbol\psi=(Y_1,\dots,Y_N,T)$~\cite{lecture10,pope1985pdf,haworth2010pdf}. An \emph{exact} transport equation can be derived for this (subgrid) PDF:
\begin{equation*}
\bar\rho\frac{\partial \tilde P_{sgs}}{\partial t} + \bar\rho\,\tilde u_j \frac{\partial \tilde P_{sgs}}{\partial x_j} - \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N}\frac{\partial}{\partial \psi_\alpha}\!\Big[\bar\rho\,\dot\omega_\alpha(\boldsymbol\psi)\,\tilde P_{sgs}\Big] = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\!\left[\left(\frac{\mu}{\sigma}+\frac{\mu_{sgs}}{\sigma_{sgs}}\right)\frac{\partial \tilde P_{sgs}}{\partial x_i}\right] - \frac{\bar\rho}{\tau_{sgs}}\sum_{\alpha=1}^{N}\frac{\partial}{\partial \psi_\alpha}\!\Big[\big(\psi_\alpha - \tilde\phi_\alpha(\mathbf{x},t)\big)\tilde P_{sgs}\Big].
\end{equation*}
Reading it term by term: the first two terms are the unsteady evolution of the PDF and its \textbf{transport in physical space} by the resolved velocity (convection); the third is \textbf{transport in composition space by chemical reaction}, and it is the decisive feature --- it appears in \emph{closed form}, so no matter how non-linear the chemistry, the reaction term requires \emph{no} turbulence--chemistry-interaction model. On the right-hand side, the first term models the \textbf{turbulent transport in physical space} by the unresolved velocity fluctuations (gradient diffusion with molecular $\mu/\sigma$ plus subgrid $\mu_{sgs}/\sigma_{sgs}$ diffusivity), and the last term is the \textbf{molecular mixing (micromixing)} term in composition space, here closed with an IEM/LMSE linear-relaxation model that relaxes each scalar towards its local mean $\tilde\phi_\alpha$ over a mixing time $\tau_{sgs}$. The great \emph{advantage} of the method is therefore the exact treatment of reaction; its \emph{drawbacks} are the high dimensionality (one extra dimension per scalar), which forces solution by Lagrangian Monte-Carlo particles ($d\phi^n/dt = \dot w^n + \mathrm{MIX}$, with $\sim$20--50 particles per cell) or, more cheaply and LES-compatibly, by 4--8 \emph{stochastic (Eulerian) fields}~\cite{lecture10,jones2014les}, plus the fact that the micromixing term remains \emph{unclosed} and is the model's main weakness. The price of generality is cost: next to DNS these are the most expensive methods, but unlike flamelet models they make no assumption about the combustion regime and naturally handle finite-rate effects, extinction and slow chemistry (NO$_x$, CO).
}

\subsubsection{Explain the main closure problems that arise when averaging the transport equations for an exothermic reacting flow.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (Turbulent Reacting Flows); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}
\label{Q:clouseure}
\paragraph{Answer}

Previous answer: it is the average of the reaction rate $\omega$ in the mass equation. The exponential terms make variations increasingly important.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
When the instantaneous balance equations are averaged (RANS) or filtered (LES), every \emph{non-linear} term produces a correlation of fluctuations that cannot be expressed through the mean/resolved variables alone --- these are the \textbf{closure (unclosed) terms}, and the averaged equations end up with more unknowns than equations~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,veynante2002turbulent,roekaerts2010jmbc}. For an exothermic reacting flow they fall into a few groups. \textbf{(1) Turbulent momentum transport:} averaging the convective term of the momentum equation yields the \textbf{Reynolds (Favre) stresses} $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' u_j''}$ --- the same closure problem as in non-reacting turbulence, usually treated with an eddy-viscosity ($k$--$\varepsilon$) model. \textbf{(2) Turbulent scalar transport:} the convective terms of the species and enthalpy equations give the \textbf{turbulent scalar fluxes} $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' Y_k''}$ and $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' h''}$; these are normally modelled by gradient diffusion ($-D_T\,\partial\tilde Y_k/\partial x_i$), but in premixed flames heat release can drive \textbf{counter-gradient transport}, where the flux points \emph{up} the mean gradient and the gradient model fails. \textbf{(3) The mean reaction rate} $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$ is the central and most severe closure problem, and the one specific to combustion: because the Arrhenius rate depends exponentially on temperature, $\dot\omega \propto \exp(-T_{act}/T)$, even a Taylor expansion about the mean ($\overline{\dot\omega}\approx \dot\omega(\bar T)\big[1 + \text{terms in } \overline{T'^2},\,\overline{Y'T'},\dots\big]$) never closes: $\overline{\dot\omega}_k \neq \dot\omega_k(\tilde Y_k,\tilde T)$, and the high temperature sensitivity means that neglecting the fluctuation correlations produces order-of-magnitude errors~\cite{lecture10}. This is exactly why the dedicated combustion models (eddy break-up, transported PDF, flamelet/FGM, flame surface density) exist. \textbf{(4)} Two further difficulties are peculiar to the strong \textbf{density variations} of exothermic flow: the means of the (non-linear) equation of state and of the temperature-dependent molecular transport coefficients are themselves unclosed, and Reynolds averaging generates density--velocity correlations $\overline{\rho' u_i'}$. The last is the very motivation for \textbf{Favre (density-weighted) averaging}, which absorbs the density into the average and removes those explicit $\overline{\rho' u'}$ terms --- although it does \emph{not} remove the scalar-flux or reaction-rate closures, which remain the hard part.
}

\subsubsection{Density-weighted averaging, also called Favre averaging, is an alternative way of averaging leading to a specific form of the averaged transport equations. What is Favre averaging? What is this specific form? Can it be expected to be a good approach for premixed flames / for non-premixed flames?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (Favre/density-weighted averaging); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\label{Q:density}

\paragraph{Answer} Represented with a tilde ($\sim$), it is an average weighted by density. It considers density as a weight, averaging $\rho$ times the quantity divided by averaged $\rho$. More important for premixed flames because of the rapid density changes (not sure about this last statement).

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Favre (density-weighted) averaging} is averaging weighted by the local density. For any quantity $Q$ it is defined as
\begin{equation*}
\tilde Q = \frac{\overline{\rho Q}}{\bar\rho},
\end{equation*}
and the instantaneous value is decomposed as $Q = \tilde Q + Q''$, with the property $\widetilde{Q''}=0$ (though in general $\overline{Q''}\neq 0$), in contrast to conventional \textbf{Reynolds averaging} $Q=\bar Q + Q'$ with $\overline{Q'}=0$. The two differ by $\tilde Q - \bar Q = \overline{\rho' Q'}/\bar\rho$, i.e.\ they coincide only when density--scalar correlations vanish~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,veynante2002turbulent}. \textbf{Why it is used:} in a combusting flow the density varies strongly, and Reynolds-averaging the conservation equations then produces a profusion of unclosed density-fluctuation correlations (e.g.\ $\overline{\rho' u_i'}$ already appears in the mean continuity equation). Density weighting absorbs $\rho$ into the average so that these terms disappear and the averaged equations recover the \textbf{same compact form as the constant-density (incompressible) equations}. The \textbf{specific form} is, for continuity, momentum and species,
\begin{align*}
&\frac{\partial \bar\rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial }{\partial x_i}\big(\bar\rho\,\tilde u_i\big) = 0, \qquad
\frac{\partial \bar\rho\,\tilde u_j}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big(\bar\rho\,\tilde u_i\,\tilde u_j\big) = -\frac{\partial \bar p}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\Big(\overline{\tau}_{ij} - \overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' u_j''}\Big),\\
&\frac{\partial \bar\rho\,\tilde Y_k}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big(\bar\rho\,\tilde u_i\,\tilde Y_k\big) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\Big(\overline{J_{k,i}} - \overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' Y_k''}\Big) + \overline{\dot\omega}_k,
\end{align*}
i.e.\ formally identical to the laminar equations except for the new unclosed terms: the Favre Reynolds stress $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''u_j''}$, the turbulent scalar flux $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''Y_k''}$, and the mean reaction rate $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$. The price is that Favre means are \textbf{not directly measurable} (experiments yield Reynolds averages), so quantitative comparison with data requires knowledge of the density correlations. \textbf{Premixed vs.\ non-premixed:} Favre averaging is a mathematical convenience that is formally valid for \emph{both} modes, and is beneficial wherever density fluctuations are large --- which is the case across the heat-releasing front in \emph{either} case. It is therefore not really that it suits only premixed flames (correcting the draft above): what matters is whether the correlation $\overline{\rho' Q'}$ is significant, and it is in both premixed and non-premixed combustion. Its main \emph{limitation} is conceptual rather than mode-dependent --- by hiding the density fluctuations it can mask differential-diffusion and counter-gradient effects, and one must always remember that Favre and Reynolds means differ when interpreting results.
}

\subsubsection{Explain what is meant by Large Eddy Simulation of turbulent flow. Also discuss the need for subgrid models of combustion processes in the context of LES.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (LES, subgrid closures); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}


\label{Q:LES}

\paragraph{Answer} LES directly calculates the larger scales but models the dissipation (smaller scales). Subgrid models simulate the effect the unresolved scales have on the large scales.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Large-Eddy Simulation} is based on \emph{spatial filtering} rather than time/ensemble averaging: a low-pass filter of width $\Delta$ (usually the grid size) splits every field into a \textbf{resolved} part, computed directly, and a \textbf{subgrid (unresolved)} part, modelled. The rationale is the universality argument of the turbulent cascade: the large, energy-containing eddies are anisotropic, geometry-dependent and carry most of the turbulent transport, so they should be resolved, whereas the small subgrid eddies are more nearly universal and mainly dissipative, so they can be modelled far more reliably than the whole spectrum (as RANS must)~\cite{pope2000turbulent,veynante2002turbulent,lecture10}. In variable-density flows one uses \textbf{Favre filtering}, $\tilde Q = \overline{\rho Q}/\bar\rho$, and the filtered equations take the same structure as the RANS equations but with the averages reinterpreted as filtered quantities; the unclosed \textbf{subgrid stress} $\tau_{ij}^{sgs} = \overline{\rho}\big(\widetilde{u_i u_j} - \tilde u_i \tilde u_j\big)$ is closed with a (e.g.\ Smagorinsky or dynamic) eddy-viscosity model. As the filter width $\Delta\to 0$ all scales are resolved and LES tends to DNS, while $\Delta$ approaching the integral scale degrades it towards RANS. LES is more expensive than RANS but captures the unsteady, large-scale phenomena (mixing, thermo-acoustics, extinction/re-ignition) that RANS cannot.

A \textbf{subgrid combustion model is nonetheless indispensable}, for a reason specific to reacting flow: the laminar flame thickness (and even more the reaction-zone thickness) is typically $\sim 0.1$--$1$\,mm, almost always \emph{smaller than any affordable filter width} $\Delta$. The flame and its chemistry therefore live \textbf{entirely at the subgrid scale}, where they are not resolved, and the filtered reaction rate suffers the same non-linearity problem as in RANS: $\overline{\dot\omega}_k \neq \dot\omega_k(\tilde Y_k,\tilde T)$, because the filtered rate is not a function of the filtered fields~\cite{lecture10}. Resolving the flame directly would demand a DNS-fine grid, defeating the purpose of LES. Hence the effect of the unresolved turbulence on the subgrid flame --- chiefly the \textbf{subgrid wrinkling and mixing} --- must be modelled. The usual closures mirror the RANS ones, adapted to the filter: \textbf{artificially thickened flame} models broaden the flame by a factor $F$ until it is resolved on the LES mesh (so the Arrhenius rate can be used directly), correcting the lost wrinkling with an efficiency function $E$; \textbf{flame surface density} and \textbf{flamelet/FGM} models integrate the tabulated chemistry over a presumed subgrid PDF parametrised by the resolved mean and the \textbf{subgrid variance}, e.g.\ $\overline{\dot\omega} = \int_0^1 \dot\omega(c)\,P\!\big(c;\tilde c,\widetilde{c''^2}\big)\,dc$, which requires an extra equation (or algebraic model) for $\widetilde{c''^2}$; and \textbf{transported-FDF/stochastic-fields} methods solve the subgrid PDF transport equation, keeping the reaction term closed at the price of cost~\cite{lecture10,jones2014les}. In every case the subgrid model supplies the turbulence--chemistry interaction that the filter has removed.
}
