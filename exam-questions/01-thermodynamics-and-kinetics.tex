\subsubsection{Explain the different types of enthalpies and how to determine the enthalpy of a reaction.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (Thermodynamics \& Chemical Equilibrium); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4 (Thermodynamics of Combustion Processes); Lecture Notes L1\&2]}

\paragraph{Answer}
Lecture 1 is more useful: \textbf{Enthalpy of formation} is the energy needed to form 1 kg of a species at 298.15 K.

It is impossible to define an absolute value for enthalpy, therefore enthalpy values are relative quantities. The standard temperature of 298 K was chosen in preference to 0 K because of experimental difficulties with the latter. To measure these quantities a bomb calorimeter is used. In this standard, pure elements are considered to have zero enthalpy of formation. Experimentally, it is possible to determine the energy released or added to form other compounds. Hess's law states that the algebraic sum of the heat released at each stage equals the total amount of heat released when the reaction occurs directly.

\textbf{Heat of reaction} is the energy released per unit mole of reaction. It can be differentiated into a higher and a lower value (HHV and LHV), depending on the state in which water is assumed to be at the end of combustion (vapor or liquid).

Enthalpy of fusion and enthalpy of vaporization are also types of enthalpy, but they are outside the scope of this question.

\subsubsection{What is heat capacity and why does it vary?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (Thermodynamics \& Chemical Equilibrium); Thermodynamics Recap audioslides (heat capacity, variation with T); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Lecture 1 is more useful: it is the energy needed to raise the temperature of a certain amount of matter by 1 K by adding heat.

As temperature increases, new degrees of freedom are unlocked in the molecules (i.e. they become relevant), so less of the added energy goes into increasing the translational motion of the particles, i.e. the temperature.

\subsubsection{What is adiabatic flame temperature and how does it vary with parameters like equivalence ratio, inlet temperature, pressure, fuel type, etc.?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (adiabatic flame temperature, equivalence ratio, pressure/temperature effects); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}
Adiabatic flame temperature is the temperature of the products when no heat loss occurs. It depends mostly on the energy released and the $c_p$ of the products. Each of these factors has further dependencies.

The energy released depends on the combination of fuel and oxidizer (latent heat) and their quantity ratio (equivalence ratio). Moreover, the energy released also depends on the combustion efficiency. Incomplete combustion leaves free radicals which did not release all the energy that would otherwise be obtained. Pressure is a major player in this situation. For example, in equilibrium chemistry combustion is not complete, but there is still a dependency on pressure: the reaction rates and the equilibrium composition vary with pressure. In general, Le Chatelier's principle makes the reaction shift towards the side with the fewest moles. This decreases the dissociation reactions, enabling more complete combustion. Lastly, the inlet temperature also affects the energy release: due to Le Chatelier's principle, higher temperatures are less favorable for exothermic reactions, decreasing the energy release.

The $c_p$ of the reactants is also affected by the equivalence ratio, pressure, and inlet temperature. An increase in dissociation, affected by pressure, and the formation of soot and other molecules also increases $c_p$. The equivalence ratio also affects $c_p$ through the extra air or extra fuel present. Finally, $c_p$ also increases at higher temperatures, so the inlet temperature affects this factor too.

Overall, the temperature increases with increasing pressure, and the maximum temperature occurs close to $\phi = 1$, with a tendency towards the rich side.




\subsubsection{What is Gibbs energy and how does it define spontaneity?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (chemical equilibrium and free energy); Thermodynamics Recap audioslides (second law, entropy); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}

It is a function addressing both the energy used to increase entropy and the energy transferred as heat. It is a quantity that decreases when the system is not in equilibrium and is constant otherwise. It represents an excess quantity. Helmholtz free energy is its equivalent for isochoric processes.
