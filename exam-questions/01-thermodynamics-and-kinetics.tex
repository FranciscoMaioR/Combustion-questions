\subsection{Thermodynamics and Kinetics}

\subsubsection{Explain different types of combustion.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 4 (Basics of Flames -- classification of combustion/flame types); Maas notes Ch.1 / Warnatz Ch.1 (Fundamental Definitions and Phenomena)]}

\paragraph{Answer}
\textbf{Fuel and oxidizer mixing:} Non-premixed and premixed. \textbf{Nature of flow:} Laminar or turbulent. \textbf{Phase of reactants:} Homogeneous and non-homogeneous. \textbf{Temporal variation:} Steady or unsteady. \textbf{Mode of combustion:} Propagation or explosion. \textbf{Speed of flow:} Compressible or incompressible.


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{exam-questions/01-thermodynamics-and-kinetics/flame_types.png}
    \caption{Classification of combustion processes by mixing of fuel and oxidizer, nature of flow, phase of reactants, temporal variation, mode of combustion, and flow speed.}
    \label{fig:flame-types}
\end{figure}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Combustion processes can be classified along several independent axes~\cite{lecture4,warnatz2006combustion}. The most fundamental distinction concerns the \textbf{mixing of fuel and oxidizer}. In \textbf{premixed combustion} the reactants are mixed at the molecular level before reaction is initiated; the flame is a thin propagating front resulting from a balance between molecular diffusion and chemical reaction, advancing into the unburnt mixture with the laminar burning velocity $s_L$. The initial state is an explosive mixture, which raises safety concerns (flashback). In \textbf{non-premixed (diffusion) combustion} fuel and oxidizer enter the reaction zone as separate streams; diffusive transport is essential to bring the reactants together, the flame sits where mixing is locally stoichiometric, and there is no intrinsic propagation speed --- the burning rate is mixing-controlled. \textbf{Partially premixed combustion} lies in between (imperfect mixing devices, spray combustion, ignition delay allowing partial mixing before reaction) and exhibits local flame propagation with a variable flame speed.

According to the \textbf{nature of the flow}, combustion is \textbf{laminar} (smooth flame fronts, transport by molecular diffusion only, e.g.\ a candle or Bunsen flame) or \textbf{turbulent} (eddies wrinkle and strain the flame, increasing the flame surface area and possibly disrupting the front into pockets; transport is greatly enhanced). According to the \textbf{phase of the reactants}, combustion is \textbf{homogeneous} (single phase, e.g.\ gaseous fuel and air) or \textbf{non-homogeneous/heterogeneous} (multiple phases, e.g.\ liquid sprays, coal particles, soot), where evaporation or surface reactions add extra rate-limiting steps.

Regarding the \textbf{mode of combustion}, one distinguishes \textbf{propagation} from \textbf{explosion}. In zero-dimensional homogeneous systems (mixing time $\ll$ chemical time) the whole mixture reacts essentially simultaneously: auto-ignition/explosion. When transport phenomena matter, a localized reaction front propagates through the mixture. Two propagation modes exist, identified as the two physical branches of the \textbf{Hugoniot curve}~\cite{lecture4,glassman2014combustion}: \textbf{deflagration}, a subsonic, nearly isobaric (slight pressure drop) wave driven by diffusion of heat and radicals into the fresh gases; and \textbf{detonation}, a supersonic wave in which a leading shock compresses and ignites the mixture, with a large pressure increase across the front. This connects to the classification by \textbf{speed of flow}: deflagrations can usually be treated as \textbf{incompressible} (low Mach number), whereas detonations are inherently \textbf{compressible}. Finally, combustion can be \textbf{steady} (e.g.\ a burner-stabilised flame, statistically stationary gas-turbine combustion) or \textbf{unsteady} (ignition, extinction, thermoacoustic oscillations, IC-engine combustion).

Further classification criteria mentioned in the lecture include heat transfer (\textbf{adiabatic} vs.\ \textbf{non-adiabatic}, important for burner-stabilised flames which lose heat to the burner), natural vs.\ forced convection (buoyancy-driven candle flames vs.\ momentum-driven jet flames, including zero-gravity behaviour), spatial dependence (0-D homogeneous reactors vs.\ 1,2,3-D systems), and the treatment of the chemistry (\textbf{equilibrium}, i.e.\ infinitely fast, vs.\ \textbf{finite-rate} chemistry). Practical devices combine several of these: an aero-engine combustor burns a turbulent, non-homogeneous (spray), partially premixed, essentially isobaric deflagration.
}


\subsubsection{Explain the different types of enthalpies and how to determine the enthalpy of a reaction.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (Thermodynamics \& Chemical Equilibrium); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4 (Thermodynamics of Combustion Processes); Lecture Notes L1\&2]}

\paragraph{Answer}
Lecture 1 is more useful: \textbf{Enthalpy of formation} is the energy needed to form 1 kg of a species at 298.15 K.

It is impossible to define an absolute value for enthalpy, therefore enthalpy values are relative quantities. The standard temperature of 298 K was chosen in preference to 0 K because of experimental difficulties with the latter. To measure these quantities a bomb calorimeter is used. In this standard, pure elements are considered to have zero enthalpy of formation. Experimentally, it is possible to determine the energy released or added to form other compounds. Hess's law states that the algebraic sum of the heat released at each stage equals the total amount of heat released when the reaction occurs directly.

\textbf{Heat of reaction} is the energy released per unit mole of reaction. It can be differentiated into a higher and a lower value (HHV and LHV), depending on the state in which water is assumed to be at the end of combustion (vapor or liquid).

Enthalpy of fusion and enthalpy of vaporization are also types of enthalpy, but they are outside the scope of this question.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{exam-questions/01-thermodynamics-and-kinetics/enthalpy_reaction.png}
    \caption{Hess's law diagram relating the enthalpies of formation of reactants and products, via the gaseous atoms, to the enthalpy of reaction.}
    \label{fig:enthalpy-reaction}
\end{figure}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Enthalpy is defined as $H \equiv U + pV$ and, being a combination of state functions, is itself a \textbf{state function}: its change between two states is path-independent. Its engineering relevance follows from the first law: for an isobaric process $\delta q = dh$, so in nearly-isobaric combustion (deflagration, the regime of virtually all propulsion devices) heat exchange is quantified directly by enthalpy changes, without needing the chemical kinetics~\cite{Langella_LectureNotes}.

Since no absolute enthalpy can be defined, all values are referred to a convention. The \textbf{standard enthalpy of formation} $\Delta h_f^0$ of a compound is the enthalpy change when 1 mole (or 1 kg) of the substance is formed from its constituent elements, with all substances in their \textbf{standard states} (1 bar, $T_0 = 298.15$ K; chosen over 0 K for experimental practicality). By convention, elements in their most stable pure form (e.g.\ \ce{O2}, \ce{N2}, \ce{H2}, graphite) have $\Delta h_f^0 = 0$; e.g.\ for \ce{CO2}, $\Delta h_f^0$ is the enthalpy change of $\ce{C(s) + O2(g) -> CO2(g)}$. Formation enthalpies are measured calorimetrically (bomb calorimeter) or obtained spectroscopically/statistically, and exploiting \textbf{Hess's law} --- the algebraic sum of heats evolved in separate stages equals the heat of the direct reaction, a direct consequence of $h$ being a state function --- they can be combined to obtain values for species whose formation reaction cannot be run directly~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,maas_combustion_lecture}.

The \textbf{absolute (total) enthalpy} of a species is the sum of its chemical and thermal parts, $h_k(T) = \Delta h_{f,k}^0 + \int_{T_0}^{T} c_{p,k}(T')\,dT'$, where the integral is the \textbf{sensible enthalpy}. For a mixture, $h_{\mathrm{mix}} = \sum_k Y_k h_k$. Other enthalpy types account for phase change: the \textbf{latent enthalpy of vaporization} and the \textbf{enthalpy of fusion}, relevant e.g.\ for liquid fuels and for the state of water in the products.

The \textbf{enthalpy (heat) of reaction} $\Delta H_R$ is the heat that must be removed (exothermic) or added (endothermic) during the reaction to bring the products back to the reactant temperature --- the constant-$T$ requirement is what makes the definition unique. From Hess's law it follows directly from formation enthalpies:
\begin{equation*}
(\Delta Q_R)_p = \Delta H_R = \big(\Delta H_{f,\mathrm{prod}}^0 - \Delta H_{f,\mathrm{reac}}^0\big) + \int_{T_0}^{T_{\mathrm{reac}}} \Delta C_p(T')\,dT',
\end{equation*}
where the second term (\textbf{Kirchhoff's law}) corrects for reactants not at standard temperature; it reflects that energy is packed differently in reactants and products at different temperatures, and is usually small. At standard conditions only the difference of formation enthalpies remains (cf.\ Fig.~\ref{fig:enthalpy-reaction}). For exothermic reactions the products have lower formation enthalpy than the reactants. Finally, because product water may be counted as vapor or liquid, one distinguishes the \textbf{higher heating value} (HHV, includes the latent heat recovered by condensing \ce{H2O}) from the \textbf{lower heating value} (LHV, water remains vapor); the LHV is the relevant one for combustion chambers, where residence times do not allow condensation~\cite{Langella_LectureNotes,glassman2014combustion}.
}


\subsubsection{What is heat capacity and why does it vary?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (Thermodynamics \& Chemical Equilibrium); Thermodynamics Recap audioslides (heat capacity, variation with T); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Lecture 1 is more useful: it is the energy needed to raise the temperature of a certain amount of matter by 1 K by adding heat.

As temperature increases, new degrees of freedom are unlocked in the molecules (i.e. they become relevant), so less of the added energy goes into increasing the translational motion of the particles, i.e. the temperature.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{exam-questions/01-thermodynamics-and-kinetics/cv.png}
    \caption{Variation of the molar heat capacity at constant volume with temperature, showing the contributions from translational, rotational, and vibrational degrees of freedom.}
    \label{fig:cv-temperature}
\end{figure}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Heat capacity is defined as the heat required to raise the temperature of a system by 1 K, $C = \delta Q / dT$; per unit mass this is the specific heat capacity $c = \delta q/dT$ (per mole, $\bar{C} = W c$). Since heat $Q$ is not a state function, $c$ only becomes a well-defined function of state once the transformation is specified; the two cases of practical interest are \textbf{constant pressure}, $c_p = (\delta q / dT)_p$, and \textbf{constant volume}, $c_v = (\delta q / dT)_V$. Physically, $c_v$ describes how internal energy varies with temperature ($du = c_v\,dT$ for a perfect gas), i.e.\ how energy is stored in the gas, while $c_p$ additionally accounts for the expansion work done at constant pressure; for a perfect gas $c_p = c_v + R$. The ratio $\gamma = c_p/c_v$ follows directly~\cite{Langella_LectureNotes}.

The variation with \textbf{temperature} is explained by the molecular \textbf{degrees of freedom} over which energy is equipartitioned. A monoatomic gas can store energy only in the three translational modes, giving $\bar{C}_v = \tfrac{3}{2}R_0$ independent of temperature. A diatomic molecule additionally activates two \textbf{rotational} modes at moderate temperatures ($\bar{C}_v \to \tfrac{5}{2}R_0$) and, at high temperature, \textbf{vibrational} modes ($\bar{C}_v \to \tfrac{7}{2}R_0$); these are quantised and only become accessible ("unlocked") when $kT$ is comparable to their energy spacing. Triatomic and larger molecules possess more rotational and vibrational modes, which moreover get excited at lower temperatures, so their heat capacity is larger and rises faster (cf.\ Fig.~\ref{fig:cv-temperature})~\cite{Langella_LectureNotes,warnatz2006combustion}. Since temperature measures only the chaotic \textbf{translational} motion, when new internal modes open up an increasing share of any added heat is stored internally rather than as translational kinetic energy --- hence more heat is needed per kelvin and $c$ increases monotonically with $T$.

Heat capacity also varies with \textbf{composition}: for a mixture $c_{p,\mathrm{mix}} = \sum_k Y_k c_{p,k}$, so during combustion the changing species mass fractions change $c_p$ even at fixed temperature (e.g.\ products rich in triatomic \ce{CO2} and \ce{H2O} have higher $c_p$ than the reactants). This double dependence $c_p(T, Y_k)$ is precisely why the adiabatic flame temperature depends on equivalence ratio, dilution and dissociation, and why accurate $T_{ad}$ calculations require polynomial fits (e.g.\ NASA polynomials) for $c_{p,k}(T)$ rather than constant values.
}


\subsubsection{What is adiabatic flame temperature and how does it vary with parameters like equivalence ratio, inlet temperature, pressure, fuel type, etc.?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (adiabatic flame temperature, equivalence ratio, pressure/temperature effects); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}
Adiabatic flame temperature is the temperature of the products when no heat loss occurs. It depends mostly on the energy released and the $c_p$ of the products. Each of these factors has further dependencies.

The energy released depends on the combination of fuel and oxidizer (latent heat) and their quantity ratio (equivalence ratio). Moreover, the energy released also depends on the combustion efficiency. Incomplete combustion leaves free radicals which did not release all the energy that would otherwise be obtained. Pressure is a major player in this situation. For example, in equilibrium chemistry combustion is not complete, but there is still a dependency on pressure: the reaction rates and the equilibrium composition vary with pressure. In general, Le Chatelier's principle makes the reaction shift towards the side with the fewest moles. This decreases the dissociation reactions, enabling more complete combustion. Lastly, the inlet temperature also affects the energy release: due to Le Chatelier's principle, higher temperatures are less favorable for exothermic reactions, decreasing the energy release.

The $c_p$ of the reactants is also affected by the equivalence ratio, pressure, and inlet temperature. An increase in dissociation, affected by pressure, and the formation of soot and other molecules also increases $c_p$. The equivalence ratio also affects $c_p$ through the extra air or extra fuel present. Finally, $c_p$ also increases at higher temperatures, so the inlet temperature affects this factor too.

Overall, the temperature increases with increasing pressure, and the maximum temperature occurs close to $\phi = 1$, with a tendency towards the rich side.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The \textbf{adiabatic flame temperature} $T_{ad}$ is the temperature reached by the products when combustion occurs without heat losses. For the isobaric case (the relevant one for gas turbines) it follows from enthalpy conservation, $h_{\mathrm{prod}}(T_{ad}) = h_{\mathrm{reac}}(T_{\mathrm{reac}})$, with $h = \sum_i Y_i \big[\Delta h_{f,i}^0 + \int_{T_0}^{T} c_{p,i}\,dT'\big]$: the chemical energy released is entirely used to heat the products. Under the simplest assumptions (complete, one-step, infinitely fast chemistry) this yields the explicit estimate~\cite{lecture2}
\begin{equation*}
T_{ad} = T_0 + \frac{Y_{F}\,\Delta H_R}{c_{p,p}\,\nu_F W_F} + \frac{c_{p,r}}{c_{p,p}}\,(T_r - T_0) \quad (\phi \le 1),
\end{equation*}
with the fuel mass fraction replaced by the oxidizer one for $\phi > 1$ (the deficient reactant limits heat release). This already shows the two governing factors: the \textbf{energy released per unit mass of mixture} and the \textbf{heat capacity of the products}.

\textbf{Equivalence ratio:} $T_{ad}$ peaks near $\phi = 1$, where neither excess air nor excess fuel acts as inert ballast absorbing heat. The peak actually lies slightly \textbf{rich} ($\phi \approx 1.05$--$1.1$): on the rich side the product mixture contains more \ce{CO} and \ce{H2} and less \ce{CO2}/\ce{H2O}, so the mean product $c_p$ is lower, and dissociation losses (endothermic) are reduced because less \ce{CO2} is formed in the first place; the heat release falls off more slowly than the lean-side dilution effect. Far from stoichiometry, $T_{ad}$ drops steeply on both sides.

\textbf{Pressure:} complete-combustion analysis gives no pressure dependence, but with equilibrium chemistry $T_{ad}$ \textbf{increases with pressure}. Dissociation reactions (e.g.\ $\ce{CO2 <=> CO + 1/2 O2}$, $\ce{H2O <=> H2 + 1/2 O2}$) increase the number of moles; by \textbf{Le Chatelier's principle} higher pressure shifts equilibrium towards the side with fewer moles, i.e.\ suppresses dissociation. Since dissociation is endothermic, suppressing it leaves more energy as sensible heat, raising $T_{ad}$ (formally, $\prod (p_k/p^0)^{\nu_k} = K_p(T)$ with $\sum \nu_k = \Delta n > 0$ forces the mole fractions of dissociation products down as $p$ rises). The effect saturates at high $p$ and is strongest near stoichiometry, where temperatures, and hence dissociation, are largest~\cite{lecture2,glassman2014combustion}.

\textbf{Inlet temperature:} preheating the reactants raises $T_{ad}$, but by \textbf{less than the preheat increment}: the products have a higher $c_p$ than the reactants ($c_{p,r}/c_{p,p} < 1$ in the relation above), and the higher final temperature enhances endothermic dissociation, which absorbs part of the additional sensible energy.

\textbf{Fuel type and oxidizer:} the relevant quantity is the heating value per unit mass of \textbf{mixture} combined with the product $c_p$, not the heating value of the fuel alone --- which is why most stoichiometric hydrocarbon/air flames cluster around 2200--2400 K (e.g.\ \ce{CH4} 2263 K, \ce{C3H8} 2290 K)~\cite{lecture2}, while \ce{C2H4} and \ce{C2H2} burn hotter due to their positive/less negative formation enthalpies. Removing the \ce{N2} ballast has a dramatic effect: \ce{H2}/air gives $\approx 2380$ K but \ce{H2}/\ce{O2} $\approx 3083$ K~\cite{maas_combustion_lecture}. Finally, for an \textbf{isochoric} process $T_{ad}$ is higher than for the isobaric case, since no energy is spent on expansion work ($du = 0$ instead of $dh = 0$, and $c_v < c_p$).
}


\subsubsection{What is Gibbs energy and how does it define spontaneity?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 2 (chemical equilibrium and free energy); Thermodynamics Recap audioslides (second law, entropy); Maas notes Ch.4 / Warnatz Ch.4]}

\paragraph{Answer}

It is a function addressing both the energy used to increase entropy and the energy transferred as heat. It is a quantity that decreases when the system is not in equilibrium and is constant otherwise. It represents an excess quantity. Helmholtz free energy is its equivalent for isochoric processes.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The second law states that for a spontaneous process the entropy of the universe increases, $\Delta S_{\mathrm{universe}} = \Delta S_{\mathrm{system}} + \Delta S_{\mathrm{surroundings}} > 0$, but this criterion is impractical because $\Delta S_{\mathrm{surroundings}}$ is unknown. Gibbs' insight was that, at \textbf{constant temperature and pressure}, the entropy change of the surroundings can be expressed through the heat exchanged by the system: $\Delta S_{\mathrm{surr}} = \Delta Q_{\mathrm{surr}}/T = -\Delta H_{\mathrm{system}}/T$ (first law, isobaric process). Substituting into the second law and rearranging gives a spontaneity criterion expressed in \textbf{system properties only}~\cite{lecture2}:
\begin{equation*}
\Delta G \equiv \Delta H - T\Delta S < 0,
\end{equation*}
where $G = H - TS$ is the \textbf{Gibbs free energy}, a state function. It represents the "free" (available) energy of the system: of the total energy, the fraction $TS$ is tied up in entropy and cannot be converted to useful work, so $\Delta G$ is the excess energy liberated under non-equilibrium conditions. For \textbf{isochoric} processes the same role is played by the \textbf{Helmholtz free energy} $F = U - TS$.

\textbf{Spontaneity:} if $\Delta G < 0$ the process is \textbf{exergonic} and proceeds spontaneously in the forward direction; if $\Delta G > 0$ it is \textbf{endergonic} and proceeds spontaneously in reverse; if $\Delta G = 0$ the system is in (dynamic) \textbf{equilibrium} --- $G$ is at a minimum and decreases monotonically whenever the system is out of equilibrium. Because $\Delta G$ combines $\Delta H$ and $-T\Delta S$, spontaneity is generally \textbf{temperature dependent} and is not simply tied to exothermicity: a reaction with $\Delta H < 0$, $\Delta S > 0$ is spontaneous at all $T$; with $\Delta H > 0$, $\Delta S > 0$ (e.g.\ dissociation, $\ce{N2 -> N + N}$) only at high $T$, where the $T\Delta S$ term dominates; with $\Delta H < 0$, $\Delta S < 0$ only at low $T$; and with $\Delta H > 0$, $\Delta S < 0$ never. This is why endothermic dissociation reactions become increasingly important at flame temperatures.

The practical power of $G$ in combustion is that it determines the \textbf{chemical equilibrium composition} without any kinetic information. For an ideal gas at constant $T$, $dG = V dp$ integrates to $G = G^0 + nR_0T \ln(p/p^0)$; applying $\Delta G_R = 0$ to a reacting mixture yields the law of mass action~\cite{lecture2,warnatz2006combustion},
\begin{equation*}
\prod_{k=1}^{N} \left(\frac{p_k}{p^0}\right)^{\nu_k} = K_p = e^{-\Delta G_R^0 / (R_0 T)},
\end{equation*}
with $K_p = K_c (R T)^{\Delta n}$. Together with element conservation and the enthalpy balance, this fixes the equilibrium products and the adiabatic flame temperature of the previous question.
}
