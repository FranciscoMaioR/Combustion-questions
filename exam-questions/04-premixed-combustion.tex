{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9 (Laminar and Turbulent Flames); Maas notes Ch.7.1, 7.2, 7.4 / Warnatz Ch.3 \& Ch.7 (Mathematical Description / Laminar Premixed Flames)]}

The final zone has little reaction, mostly oxidation and slow reactions.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
A flat (one-dimensional) laminar premixed flame is the canonical reference flame: a stream of \textbf{premixed} fuel and oxidiser flows into a stationary planar front and emerges as hot burnt products. On a flat-flame (porous-plate / McKenna) burner the flame is held stationary because the unburnt-gas velocity equals the laminar burning velocity, $v_u = s_L$~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. Moving in the flame-normal direction, the structure consists of \textbf{three zones}~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,glassman2014combustion,lecture9}. \textbf{(1) Preheat (convective--diffusive) zone:} the incoming reactants are heated, almost entirely by \textbf{heat conduction} from downstream, with negligible reaction; this is where the temperature rises gradually and the gradient is set by the balance of convection and diffusion. Its width defines the \textbf{thermal (diffusive) flame thickness} $\delta = \lambda_u/(\rho_u c_p s_L) = \alpha/s_L$, and a practical measure is $\delta_L^0 = (T_b - T_u)/|\partial T/\partial x|_{\max}$~\cite{lecture9}. \textbf{(2) Inner reaction zone:} a \textbf{thin} layer (an order of magnitude thinner than the preheat zone, $\delta_r \approx \delta/10$) where the fuel is consumed, the chain-branching radical pool is built up and the bulk of the \textbf{heat release} occurs; here the temperature gradient and reaction rate are largest. \textbf{(3) Oxidation / post-flame (recombination) zone:} a slower region dominated by three-body radical recombination and CO oxidation ($\mathrm{CO}\!\rightarrow\!\mathrm{CO_2}$), in which the gas relaxes towards chemical equilibrium and the adiabatic flame temperature $T_b$. Because of the heat release the density falls across the flame by $\rho_u/\rho_b = T_b/T_u$, so the flow is \textbf{accelerated} (chemical conversion $\rightarrow$ heat release $\rightarrow$ density change $\rightarrow$ acceleration)~\cite{lecture9}. The progress of reaction is conveniently described by a \textbf{progress variable} $c = (Y_k - Y_{k,u})/(Y_{k,b}-Y_{k,u})$ or $\theta = (T-T_u)/(T_b-T_u)$, running from $0$ (unburnt) to $1$ (burnt). A scaling estimate of the burning velocity follows from balancing diffusion and chemistry in the front, $s_L^0 \propto \sqrt{\alpha/\tau_{chem}}$, and $\delta_L^0 \approx 2\delta(1+\tau)^{0.7}$ with $\tau = T_{ad}/T_u - 1$~\cite{lecture9}. The picture above strictly holds for unity Lewis number; when $\mathrm{Le}\neq 1$ (e.g.\ hydrogen) the thermal and species profiles no longer coincide and the flame speed and structure are modified by \textbf{differential diffusion}~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,lecture8}.
}

\paragraph{Claude answer}
The construction of the dimensionless groups ($\mathrm{Re}_t$, $\mathrm{Da}$, $\mathrm{Ka}$), the regime boundaries and the meaning of each regime were treated in the turbulent time-scales question of the Turbulent Transport section, including the Borghi--Peters diagram itself (Fig.~\ref{fig:peters-diagram}); the present answer only complements that discussion without repeating it. The \textbf{purpose} of a regime diagram is to classify turbulent premixed combustion by comparing turbulence scales with the chemical (flame) scales, so as to predict which physical picture --- and therefore which modelling strategy --- applies~\cite{peters2000turbulent,warnatz2006combustion,veynante2002turbulent}. Both classical diagrams use logarithmic axes of a \textbf{velocity ratio} $u'_{rms}/s_L^0$ and a \textbf{length ratio} $l_t/\delta$, on which the lines $\mathrm{Re}_t=1$, $\mathrm{Da}=1$ and $\mathrm{Ka}=1$ are straight.

The \textbf{Borghi diagram}~\cite{borghi1985structure} was the original; the \textbf{Peters diagram}~\cite{peters2000turbulent} is its refinement, and the two are usually shown merged. The key conceptual additions worth highlighting (beyond the time-scales answer) are: (i) the $\mathrm{Ka}=1$ line is the \textbf{Klimov--Williams criterion}, marking where the smallest (Kolmogorov) eddies become as thin as the flame preheat zone and can start to penetrate it~\cite{peters2000turbulent,roekaerts2010jmbc}; (ii) Peters separates the old "distributed reaction zone" region into a \textbf{thin reaction zones} regime ($1<\mathrm{Ka}<100$, also written $\mathrm{Ka}_\delta<1$), in which small eddies thicken the preheat zone but the much thinner inner reaction layer (thickness $\delta_r\approx\delta/10$) survives, and a \textbf{broken/distributed reaction zones} regime beyond $\mathrm{Ka}_\delta=1$ (i.e.\ $\mathrm{Ka}\approx100$ for methane), where even the inner layer is disrupted, leading to local extinction; and (iii) the \textbf{corrugated vs.\ wrinkled flamelet} boundary at $u'_{rms}/s_L^0=1$. Both diagrams rest on strong idealisations --- homogeneous isotropic turbulence, single-step chemistry, unity Lewis number, and the equilibrium scaling between $u'$ and $\varepsilon$ --- so the boundaries are order-of-magnitude indicators rather than sharp lines; differential diffusion ($\mathrm{Le}\neq1$, hydrogen) in particular shifts them and can sustain flamelet behaviour well into nominally "broken" regions~\cite{veynante2002turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}.

\subsubsection{What is laminar flame speed? What is turbulent flame speed? How are they related?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9; Maas notes Ch.7 / Warnatz Ch.8.3 (Flame velocities) \& Ch.15]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Laminar flame speed, or displacement speed, is related to the consumption of products. It is the velocity of the flame with respect to the reactants.

Turbulent flame speed is the average rate of
propagation of the flame through the turbulent premixed gas mixture.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.2\linewidth]{exam-questions/04-premixed-combustion/StSl.png}
    \caption{Relation between turbulent and laminar flame speeds, $S_T/S_L = \sqrt{D_T/\alpha}$, in terms of the turbulent diffusivity $D_T$ and the molecular diffusivity $\alpha$.}
    \label{fig:turbulent-flame-speed-ratio}
\end{figure}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The \textbf{laminar flame speed} (laminar burning velocity) $s_L^0$ is the propagation speed of a planar, adiabatic, unstretched, freely-propagating premixed flame relative to the unburnt gas. It is an \textbf{eigenvalue of the reaction--diffusion problem} and a genuine property of the mixture, fixed by equivalence ratio, unburnt temperature, pressure and fuel; the scaling $s_L^0 \propto \sqrt{\alpha/\tau_{chem}} \propto \sqrt{\alpha\,\dot\omega}$ shows it is set by the balance of diffusion and chemistry~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. It peaks at (slightly rich of) stoichiometric and varies strongly with fuel ($s_L^0$ of \ce{H2} is several times that of hydrocarbons)~\cite{lecture9}. Two precise definitions must be distinguished~\cite{lecture9}: the \textbf{displacement speed} $s_d = (\vec w - \vec v)\cdot \vec n = \tfrac{1}{|\nabla c|}(\partial_t + \vec v\cdot\nabla)c$ (kinematic speed of the front iso-surface relative to the fresh gas, which depends on diffusivities and hence differs for different $c$-isolines, especially when $\mathrm{Le}\neq1$), and the \textbf{consumption speed} $s_c = -\tfrac{1}{\rho_u Y_{f,u}}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\dot\omega_f\,dn$ (based on the integrated fuel burning rate). For an unstretched planar flame they coincide and equal $s_L^0$; stretch $K$ (strain $+$ curvature) makes them differ, $s_L = s_L^0 - \mathcal{L}K$, with $\mathcal{L}$ the Markstein length~\cite{Law1988,lecture9}.

The \textbf{turbulent flame speed} (turbulent burning velocity) $s_T$ is defined as the \emph{average} rate of propagation of the flame brush through the turbulent premixed mixture~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. Unlike $s_L^0$ it is \textbf{not a mixture property}: it depends on the turbulence (chiefly $u'_{rms}$ and $l_t$) and on the configuration. The dominant mechanism is the \textbf{increase of flame-surface area} by turbulent wrinkling: in the flamelet regimes the brush still burns locally at $s_L$ but over a larger, convoluted area, so $s_T/s_L \approx A_T/A_L$. Asymptotic/scaling results give $s_T \approx s_L(1 + u'/s_L)$ for moderate turbulence and $s_T \approx u'$ for $u'\gg s_L$, while the turbulent-diffusivity analogy with the laminar estimate gives $s_T/s_L = \sqrt{D_T/\alpha}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:turbulent-flame-speed-ratio})~\cite{lecture9}. At high turbulence the curve does not keep rising: the \textbf{bending effect} (sub-linear growth) and eventually a \textbf{quenching} regime appear, as intense small-scale stretch causes local extinction~\cite{warnatz2006combustion}. Empirical correlations capture this as $s_T/s_L = b_1 + b_2 (u'/s_L)^{b_3}(l_t/\delta)^{b_4}\mathrm{Le}^{b_5}$, with the Lewis number of the deficient reactant entering explicitly~\cite{ciccarelli2008flame}. \textbf{Relation:} $s_T$ reduces to $s_L$ in the limit $u'\to 0$, and $s_L^0$ remains the fundamental building block --- the local burning rate that, multiplied by the turbulence-generated surface area, yields $s_T$. This is precisely the idea formalised by turbulent flame-speed closure (next question).
}

\subsubsection{How can a laminar premixed flame be described in an intrinsic way using a so-called progress variable? How can a turbulent premixed flame be described using a progress variable?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9/10; Warnatz Ch.15 (progress variable, premixed turbulent combustion); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer}
For a premixed flame the progress variable is $C$, relating the current quantity of a species to its final quantity.

Flames can then be shown as isolines of $C$. The gradient of $C$ can define a normal direction with respect to the flame. $C$ can be related to diffusion and consumption terms (is $S_c$ this consumption term?).
Not sure how this changes in turbulent flow, should be the same, both are premixed.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Laminar premixed flame.} A premixed flame can be described intrinsically by a single \textbf{progress variable} $c$ that maps the thermochemical state monotonically from the fresh to the burnt mixture. It is defined by normalising either a suitable species or the temperature, $c = (Y_k - Y_{k,u})/(Y_{k,b}-Y_{k,u})$ or $\theta = (T-T_u)/(T_b-T_u)$, so that $c=0$ in the unburnt gas and $c=1$ in the fully burnt gas; the species chosen must vary \textbf{monotonically} through the flame for $c$ to be single-valued~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. Confirming the draft above: the flame is then represented by \textbf{iso-surfaces} $c=c_f$ (often taken at the location of maximum reaction rate), and the unit normal pointing towards the fresh gas is $\vec n = -\nabla c/|\nabla c|$~\cite{lecture9}. The progress variable obeys a transport (reaction--diffusion--convection) equation
\begin{equation*}
\rho\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} + \vec v\cdot\nabla\right)c = -\nabla\cdot \vec J_c + \rho S_c,
\end{equation*}
so it is governed by a diffusion flux $\vec J_c$ and a \textbf{chemical source term} $S_c$ --- which is indeed the consumption/production term the draft asks about (the reaction rate of the chosen variable). The kinematic \textbf{displacement speed} of the iso-surface follows directly, $s_d = \tfrac{1}{|\nabla c|}(\partial_t + \vec v\cdot\nabla)c = \tfrac{1}{\rho|\nabla c|}\big[-\nabla\cdot\vec J_c + \rho S_c\big]$, showing that $s_d$ depends on \emph{both} the source and the diffusion --- hence on the diffusivities, which is why $s_d$ differs between species/isolines under differential diffusion (important for hydrogen)~\cite{lecture9}.

\textbf{Turbulent premixed flame.} The same variable is used, but it is \emph{not} simply "the same": one solves a Favre-averaged (RANS) or filtered (LES) transport equation for $\tilde c$,
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \bar\rho\,\tilde c}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \bar\rho\,\tilde u_i\,\tilde c}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\!\left(\overline{\rho D\frac{\partial c}{\partial x_i}}\right) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\big(\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' c''}\big) + \overline{\dot\omega}_c,
\end{equation*}
which introduces \textbf{two new unclosed terms} absent in the laminar case: the \textbf{turbulent scalar flux} $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i'' c''}$ (often counter-gradient in premixed flames) and, above all, the \textbf{mean reaction rate} $\overline{\dot\omega}_c$ that embodies the turbulence--chemistry interaction~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent}. The mean $\tilde c$ now varies smoothly across a thick turbulent \textbf{flame brush} and can be interpreted as the probability of finding burnt gas. Closing $\overline{\dot\omega}_c$ is exactly the modelling problem: in the flamelet limit one writes the Bray--Moss--Libby bimodal PDF $P(c)=\alpha\,\delta(c)+\beta\,\delta(1-c)$, or uses a flame-surface-density / flame-speed-closure model, or tabulates the chemistry against $c$ (and mixture fraction, for FGM) and integrates over a presumed PDF $P(c;\tilde c,\widetilde{c''^2})$~\cite{lecture10,peters2000turbulent,vanoijen2000fgm}. So the progress-variable concept carries over, but turbulence adds the subgrid wrinkling/mixing that must be modelled.
}
