\paragraph{Answer}

There is a small rise in temperature while the fuel and oxidizer break down.

This is followed by a thin reaction zone with a sudden rise in temperature, where radicals combine.

The final zone has little reaction, mostly oxidation and slow reactions.

\subsubsection{What is laminar flame speed? What is turbulent flame speed? How are they related?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9; Maas notes Ch.7 / Warnatz Ch.8.3 (Flame velocities) \& Ch.15]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Laminar flame speed, or displacement speed, is related to the consumption of products. It is the velocity of the flame with respect to the reactants.

Turbulent flame speed is the average rate of
propagation of the flame through the turbulent premixed gas mixture.

\subsubsection{How can a laminar premixed flame be described in an intrinsic way using a so-called progress variable? How can a turbulent premixed flame be described using a progress variable?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9/10; Warnatz Ch.15 (progress variable, premixed turbulent combustion); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer}
For a premixed flame the progress variable is $C$, relating the current quantity of a species to its final quantity.

Flames can then be shown as isolines of $C$. The gradient of $C$ can define a normal direction with respect to the flame. $C$ can be related to diffusion and consumption terms (is $S_c$ this consumption term?).
Not sure how this changes in turbulent flow, should be the same.
