The modelling-chapter emphasis is that for RANS and LES a \emph{separate} combustion closure for the mean/filtered reaction rate is required on top of the turbulence treatment --- the subject of the remaining questions in this section.

\subsubsection{Explain the main closure problems that arise when averaging the transport equations for an exothermic reacting flow.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (Turbulent Reacting Flows); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer} same as \ref{Q:clouseure}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Treated in full under Turbulent Transport (Sec.~\ref{Q:clouseure}); the same closure problems apply verbatim here, so only the headline is repeated. Averaging (RANS) or filtering (LES) the instantaneous balance equations leaves several \textbf{unclosed terms}: the \textbf{Reynolds/Favre stresses} $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''u_j''}$ (turbulent momentum transport), the \textbf{turbulent scalar fluxes} $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''Y_k''}$ and $\overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''h''}$ (which can be counter-gradient in premixed flames), and --- the one specific to combustion and by far the hardest --- the \textbf{mean reaction rate} $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$, which because of the exponential Arrhenius non-linearity is \emph{not} a function of the mean quantities, $\overline{\dot\omega}_k\neq\dot\omega_k(\tilde Y_k,\tilde T)$~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}. Strong density variation adds the unclosed mean equation of state and the density--velocity correlations that motivate Favre averaging. The combustion models discussed in this section (eddy break-up, flamelet/FGM, transported PDF, $\dots$) are precisely the different strategies for closing $\overline{\dot\omega}_k$.
}




\subsubsection{Density-weighted averaging, also called Favre averaging, is an alternative way of averaging leading to a specific form of the averaged transport equations. What is Favre averaging? What is this specific form?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (Favre/density-weighted averaging); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer} same as \ref{Q:density}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Fully covered under Turbulent Transport (Sec.~\ref{Q:density}), where the premixed/non-premixed applicability is also discussed --- so this question's narrower phrasing is entirely contained there. In short: \textbf{Favre (density-weighted) averaging} is averaging weighted by density, $\tilde Q = \overline{\rho Q}/\bar\rho$, with the decomposition $Q = \tilde Q + Q''$ and $\widetilde{Q''}=0$~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}. \textbf{Specific form:} applied to the variable-density conservation equations it removes the explicit density-fluctuation correlations (e.g.\ $\overline{\rho'u'}$) so that the averaged continuity, momentum and species equations recover the \textbf{compact, quasi-incompressible form}
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial \bar\rho}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial(\bar\rho\,\tilde u_i)}{\partial x_i} = 0, \qquad
\frac{\partial \bar\rho\,\tilde Y_k}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial(\bar\rho\,\tilde u_i\tilde Y_k)}{\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}\Big(\overline{J_{k,i}} - \overline{\rho}\,\widetilde{u_i''Y_k''}\Big) + \overline{\dot\omega}_k,
\end{equation*}
i.e.\ formally identical to the constant-density equations but with the new unclosed Favre stresses, turbulent scalar fluxes and mean reaction rate. It is the natural averaging for combustion precisely because heat release makes density vary strongly.
}




\subsubsection{Explain what is meant by Large Eddy Simulation of turbulent flow. Also discuss the need for subgrid models of combustion processes in the context of LES.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.13 (LES, subgrid closures); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer} same as \ref{Q:LES}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Answered in full under Turbulent Transport (Sec.~\ref{Q:LES}); the same content applies. Briefly: \textbf{LES} applies a spatial low-pass \textbf{filter} of width $\Delta$, computing the large, energy-containing, geometry-dependent eddies directly and modelling only the smaller, more universal \textbf{subgrid} scales (via a Smagorinsky/dynamic subgrid-stress model); as $\Delta\to0$ it tends to DNS, and as $\Delta$ grows it degrades towards RANS~\cite{lecture10,pope2000turbulent,veynante2002turbulent}. A \textbf{subgrid combustion model is indispensable} because the flame and reaction zone ($\sim$0.1--1\,mm) are almost always \emph{thinner than the filter width}, so the chemistry lives entirely at the subgrid scale and the filtered reaction rate again satisfies $\overline{\dot\omega}_k\neq\dot\omega_k(\tilde Y_k,\tilde T)$; the unresolved wrinkling/mixing must therefore be supplied by a subgrid closure (thickened flame, flame-surface-density, flamelet/FGM with a presumed subgrid PDF, or transported FDF/stochastic fields)~\cite{lecture10}.
}




\subsubsection{Define mixture fraction. Which conditions have to be satisfied in order that mixture fraction becomes a useful variable? Explain the idea that, using mixture fraction, a non-premixed flame problem can be split into a mixing problem and a chemical problem.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9/10; Maas notes Ch.13 / Warnatz Ch.14 (Turbulent Non-Premixed Flames); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz/Maas)]}

\paragraph{Answer} same as \ref{Q:mixfrac}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
This is identical to the Non-Premixed-Combustion question answered in full at Sec.~\ref{Q:mixfrac} (definition, the conditions for $Z$ to be useful, and the splitting of the problem into a mixing and a chemistry part, including the Burke--Schumann and Bilger material) --- so only the essentials are restated here. The \textbf{mixture fraction} $Z$ is the mass fraction of material originating from the fuel stream ($Z=1$ fuel inlet, $Z=0$ oxidiser inlet)~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. It is useful provided (i) a \textbf{two-feed} system with well-defined inlets, (ii) \textbf{equal diffusivities} (unity Lewis numbers) so it is a single well-defined conserved scalar, and (iii) for the flamelet picture a thin reaction zone~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical}. Its power is that it is a \textbf{conserved scalar} obeying a source-free transport equation, which \textbf{decouples} the problem into a \emph{mixing problem} (solve the turbulent transport of the passive scalar $Z$) and a \emph{chemistry problem} (the thermochemistry as a state relationship $Y_k=Y_k(Z)$ from equilibrium / Burke--Schumann / flamelet) --- the conceptual basis of all mixture-fraction-based non-premixed models in this chapter.
}




\subsubsection{Explain the structure of the strained laminar flamelet model for non-premixed combustion.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.14 (flamelet model, strained flames); Dynamics of Stretched Flames (Law, 1988, supplementary on flame stretch, verify against Warnatz); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer} same as \ref{Q:stedylaminatflameletmodel}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The \emph{steady laminar flamelet model} is described at Sec.~\ref{Q:stedylaminatflameletmodel}; the \textbf{strained} laminar flamelet is the same family of model viewed through its \textbf{controlling parameter} --- the strain, which enters as the scalar dissipation rate --- so this answer cross-references that one and focuses on the strain angle. \textbf{Structure:} the turbulent non-premixed flame is treated as an ensemble of thin laminar diffusion flamelets each of which is taken to be a \textbf{strained counterflow (opposed-jet) flame}; transforming to mixture-fraction coordinates and neglecting tangential transport gives the flamelet equations
\begin{equation*}
0 = \rho\,\frac{\chi}{2}\,\frac{\partial^2 Y_k}{\partial Z^2} + \dot\omega_k, \qquad 0 = \rho\,\frac{\chi}{2}\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial Z^2} + \dot\omega_T,
\end{equation*}
in which the \textbf{scalar dissipation rate} $\chi = 2D|\nabla Z|^2$ is the sole imprint of the surrounding flow and is \emph{directly proportional to the imposed strain rate} $a$ (in the counterflow, $\chi_{st}\sim a$)~\cite{lecture9,peters2000turbulent,poinsot2012theoretical}. Thus the flamelet library is generated by solving \textbf{strained counterflow flames over a range of strain rates}, yielding state relationships $Y_k(Z,\chi_{st})$, $T(Z,\chi_{st})$~\cite{lecture9,Law1988}. \textbf{The strain dependence is the whole point:} low strain (low $\chi_{st}$) $\to$ near-equilibrium burning; increasing strain pulls the solution off equilibrium (lower peak $T$, reactant leakage); beyond a critical $\chi_{st,q}$ the flamelet \textbf{extinguishes} (the upper branch of the S-curve). In a turbulent computation these strained-flamelet tables are combined with a presumed joint PDF $\tilde P(Z,\chi)$ (or $\tilde P(Z)$ with a model for $\chi$) to give Favre means. The \emph{steady} qualifier signals the limitation: each flamelet is assumed quasi-steady, so strongly unsteady strain, extinction/re-ignition transients and slow chemistry require \textbf{unsteady} flamelets or extra manifold dimensions~\cite{peters2000turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}.
}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
This is the same topic as Sec.~\ref{Q:Variance of mixure fraction} in the Non-Premixed section, and the draft above states the essentials correctly. In summary: the \textbf{variance} $\widetilde{Z''^2}$ is needed because it is the \textbf{second parameter} of the presumed PDF --- it sets the PDF \emph{width}, i.e.\ the degree of unmixedness, and hence directly controls the mean reaction rate (vanishing variance $\Rightarrow$ perfectly mixed $\Rightarrow \tilde\phi=\phi(\tilde Z)$)~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,peters2000turbulent}. It is obtained from its own transport equation, in which it is \textbf{produced} by the mean-gradient/turbulent-flux term and \textbf{destroyed} by molecular mixing at the rate of the \textbf{scalar dissipation rate} $\tilde\chi = 2\overline{D\,|\nabla Z''|^2}$. Thus $\chi$ is precisely the \textbf{sink of variance}: a high scalar dissipation rate rapidly smooths fluctuations, narrowing the PDF towards the mixed state, whereas low $\chi$ lets unmixedness persist. In practice the budget is closed algebraically as $\tilde\chi\approx C_\chi(\varepsilon/k)\,\widetilde{Z''^2}$ (linear relaxation), so $\chi$ and the variance are proportional through the turbulent mixing frequency; the same $\chi$ also parametrises departure from equilibrium and extinction in the flamelet equations, coupling the variance budget to the flamelet structure~\cite{lecture10,peters2000turbulent}.
}




\subsubsection{Explain how model development and experiments come together in the process of model validation. Provide some conditions for good model validation.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Lecture 5/6 (experimental methods, canonical flames); combustion\_modeling.pdf (validation against DNS/experiments, supplementary, verify against Warnatz where possible)]}

\paragraph{Answer}  Many models need empirical constants.

Experiments can determine the dependent variables.

Validation requires the same conditions.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Model development} and \textbf{validation} form a loop. Turbulent-combustion models invariably contain \textbf{closure assumptions and empirical/model constants} (e.g.\ $C_{EBU}$ in eddy break-up, the Smagorinsky/mixing constants, the $b_i$ in flame-speed correlations); these are first calibrated on simple configurations, after which the model must be tested against \emph{independent} reference data to establish its predictive credibility~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent}. The reference data come from two complementary sources: well-characterised \textbf{experiments} (laser diagnostics on canonical flames) and \textbf{DNS}, which acts as a model-free ``numerical experiment'' giving access to quantities that are hard to measure~\cite{lecture10,pope2000turbulent}. A useful distinction is between \textbf{a priori} testing (extract turbulence fields from DNS/experiment and check whether the closure reproduces the exact unclosed term) and \textbf{a posteriori} testing (run the full model and compare the predicted flow/flame with data); only the latter exposes how modelling errors accumulate and interact.

\textbf{Conditions for good validation}~\cite{lecture10,roekaerts2010jmbc,veynante2002turbulent}: (i) \textbf{well-defined, fully-specified boundary and inlet conditions} (geometry, velocity and turbulence statistics, composition, temperature) so that the simulation solves \emph{the same} problem as the experiment --- this is why standardised benchmark flames are used; (ii) comparison of quantities that genuinely \textbf{stress the model}, ideally ones it was \emph{not} tuned on, and \textbf{not} re-tuning constants per case (blind validation); (iii) \textbf{separation of numerical error from modelling error}, i.e.\ demonstrate grid/statistical convergence before attributing discrepancies to the model; (iv) \textbf{quantified uncertainty} on both the measurement and the simulation, so that ``agreement'' is judged within error bars; and (v) a \textbf{validation hierarchy} --- progress from unit problems and canonical laboratory flames (e.g.\ the TNF-workshop Sandia flames, Sec.~below) up to full devices, since agreement on a complex case is only meaningful if the building blocks are already validated. The communal benchmark approach of the \textbf{Turbulent Non-premixed Flames (TNF) workshop} exemplifies this: shared, well-documented target flames against which competing models are compared on equal footing~\cite{barlowfrank1998sandia,lecture10}.
}

They are increasing in speed.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The \textbf{Sandia Flames D, E and F} are a celebrated series of \textbf{piloted turbulent non-premixed (partially premixed) jet flames} measured by Barlow and Frank at Sandia, and are among the standard benchmark targets of the \textbf{TNF (Turbulent Non-premixed Flames) workshop}~\cite{barlowfrank1998sandia,lecture10}. }






\subsubsection{Discuss the presence and importance of stretch in the counterflow flame configuration.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9; Dynamics of Stretched Flames (Law, 1988, supplementary -- explicitly flag as unverified against infallible sources if not covered in Warnatz/Maas); Warnatz Ch.7/Ch.13 for flame structure]}

\paragraph{Answer} The most important is tangential stretch. The tangential stretch carries the radicals outwards, possibly extinguishing the flame front.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Flame stretch} is the fractional rate of change of a flame-surface element, $K = \tfrac{1}{A}\,\mathrm{d}A/\mathrm{d}t = [\nabla_t\cdot\vec w_t + (\vec w\cdot\vec n)(\nabla\cdot\vec n)]_s$, i.e.\ the sum of \textbf{tangential strain} (flow non-uniformity along the surface) and a \textbf{curvature/motion} term~\cite{Law1988,lecture9}. The \textbf{counterflow (opposed-jet) configuration} --- two opposed nozzles, fuel-vs-oxidiser for a non-premixed flame or reactants-vs-products / twin-flames for a premixed flame, with a flat flame sitting near the stagnation plane --- is the canonical way to impose a \textbf{well-defined, steady, almost uniform strain rate} $a$ on a flat flame. Because the flame is planar and aligned with the stagnation plane, the curvature term is negligible and the stretch reduces essentially to pure \textbf{tangential strain}, $K \approx a$, which is why this geometry is so important: it isolates and quantifies the effect of stretch on flame structure, burning rate and extinction with a single control parameter~\cite{lecture9,Law1988,poinsot2012theoretical}.

Its \textbf{importance} is threefold. (1) \emph{Diagnostics of stretch sensitivity}: sweeping $a$ traces the response of the flame (the classic \textbf{S-curve}) up to the \textbf{extinction strain rate}, and yields the Markstein length via $s_L = s_L^0 - \mathcal{L}K$ for premixed flames~\cite{Law1988,lecture9}. (2) \emph{Building block for flamelet/FGM modelling}: the strained counterflow flame is exactly the laminar ``flamelet'' whose solutions, parametrised by strain (equivalently the stoichiometric scalar dissipation rate $\chi_{st}\sim a$), populate the lookup manifolds used in turbulent combustion models. (3) \emph{Mechanism of extinction} --- confirming and elaborating the draft: as the strain (residence-time$^{-1}$) increases, the \textbf{tangential strain transports heat and radicals out of the thin reaction zone} faster than chemistry can replenish them; when the chemical time can no longer keep up with the reduced residence time the flame extinguishes (heat loss $+$ radical loss). A key distinction: a \textbf{premixed} flame has a propagation speed and, unless it is restrained (as it is at the counterflow stagnation plane) or subject to differential diffusion, it can simply adjust its position and ``run away'' from the strain, so stretch is only effective when the flame is held; a \textbf{non-premixed} flame has \emph{no} propagation speed, cannot escape the strain, is therefore directly and immediately affected, and extinguishes at comparatively \textbf{smaller} strain rates~\cite{lecture9,Law1988,warnatz2006combustion}.
}

\subsubsection{Provide an outline of the FGM approach to turbulent non-premixed combustion.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; combustion\_modeling.pdf (FGM/flamelet-generated manifolds, supplementary -- explicitly flag as unverified against infallible sources if not covered in Warnatz/Maas)]}

\paragraph{Answer} Not sure what FGM is.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{FGM = Flamelet-Generated Manifold} (van Oijen \& de Goey), a \textbf{tabulated-chemistry} method that fuses the \emph{flamelet} idea (the flame structure is governed by a few controlling variables) with the \emph{low-dimensional manifold} idea (the accessed thermochemical states form a low-dimensional surface in composition space)~\cite{vanoijen2000fgm,lecture10}. The thermochemical state is parametrised by a \textbf{small set of controlling variables} --- for partially premixed / non-premixed combustion typically the \textbf{mixture fraction} $Z$ (mixing) and a \textbf{reaction-progress variable} $c$ (reaction progress), with optionally enthalpy $h$ to include heat loss. The manifold $Y_k^{fgm}(Z,c)$, $T^{fgm}(Z,c)$, and the source term $S_c(Z,c)$ are \textbf{pre-computed once} with full detailed chemistry by solving representative \textbf{1-D laminar flamelets} --- either non-premixed counterflow flamelets carried towards equilibrium, or a set of premixed flamelets spanning a range of equivalence ratios --- and stored in a lookup table~\cite{lecture10,vanoijen2000fgm}. In the turbulent CFD one then solves transport equations only for the few controlling variables (and their subgrid variances), and recovers all reactive scalars by integrating the manifold over a \textbf{presumed joint PDF}:
\begin{equation*}
\tilde Y_k = \int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 Y_k^{fgm}(Z,c)\,\tilde P(Z,c)\,\mathrm{d}Z\,\mathrm{d}c .
\end{equation*}
\textbf{Outline of the approach:} (1) choose controlling variables; (2) generate the flamelet library and build the manifold; (3) integrate with the presumed PDF to make a \emph{turbulent} (Favre-averaged/filtered) table; (4) in the CFD, transport $\tilde Z$, $\tilde c$ (and variances) and look up the mean state. \textbf{Advantages:} it retrieves the accuracy of detailed chemistry at the cost of only $\sim$2--3 scalar transport equations, so it is far cheaper than transported-PDF or finite-rate approaches and naturally spans the premixed--non-premixed (partially premixed) range; it has been extended to heat loss, ignition delay and dilution by adding manifold dimensions~\cite{lecture10,vanoijen2000fgm}. \textbf{Drawbacks:} it presumes the chosen low-dimensional manifold (i.e.\ the flamelet structure) captures the chemistry, so it degrades for strongly unsteady or highly non-flamelet situations where the true state leaves the manifold, and accuracy depends on the chosen controlling variables and PDF shape~\cite{lecture10}.
}

The two methods are \textbf{conceptually opposite in what they assume and what they leave to be modelled}~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,pope1985pdf}. \textbf{Flamelet models} rest on a \textbf{separation of scales}: the reaction zone is assumed thin compared with the turbulent scales, so the turbulent flame is an ensemble of essentially \emph{laminar} flame structures. Chemistry and turbulence are thereby \textbf{decoupled} --- the thermochemistry is pre-computed as a \textbf{low-dimensional manifold} (a function of $Z$ and/or $c$, possibly parametrised by the scalar dissipation rate), and turbulence enters \emph{only} through transport and through the statistics (a presumed PDF) used to average over the manifold. The instantaneous state is \textbf{constrained to lie on that manifold}. \textbf{Transported-PDF (and filtered-density-function) methods make no such assumption}: they solve a transport equation for the joint PDF of the thermochemical state itself, in which --- crucially --- the highly non-linear \textbf{chemical source term appears in closed form} (it needs no turbulence--chemistry-interaction model, however complex the kinetics), while the term that must instead be \emph{modelled} is the \textbf{molecular mixing (micromixing)} in composition space~\cite{lecture10,pope1985pdf,haworth2010pdf}. So the ``complete difference'' is a reversal of which physics is exact and which is modelled: flamelet methods model the chemistry's coupling to turbulence by assuming a structure but treat the reaction rate as a tabulated/manifold quantity, whereas PDF methods treat the reaction rate \emph{exactly} and instead model the mixing --- with no flame-structure assumption, so they handle finite-rate effects, local extinction and re-ignition that flamelet models cannot, at much greater cost.

Addressing the question in the note above: \textbf{neither method is restricted to one combustion mode}. Flamelet models exist for \emph{premixed} flames (progress-variable / flame-surface-density), for \emph{non-premixed} flames (mixture-fraction flamelets) and for \emph{partially premixed} systems (FGM, using both $Z$ and $c$). Transported-PDF methods are \textbf{general and regime-independent} --- their whole appeal is that they make no assumption about premixed vs.\ non-premixed structure --- and are applied successfully to premixed, non-premixed and partially premixed combustion alike~\cite{lecture10,haworth2010pdf,veynante2002turbulent}. The distinction between the two families is therefore \emph{not} about combustion mode but about whether a low-dimensional manifold (flamelet) assumption is made or not.
}

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The recommendation follows directly from the regime classification and the model--regime association built up at Sec.~\ref{Q: models peters} (Borghi--Peters diagram); the draft's criterion is on the right track. \textbf{Flamelet models are recommended when the flamelet assumption holds} --- a thin reaction zone relative to the turbulence, i.e.\ \textbf{high Damk\"ohler number} and \textbf{low-to-moderate Karlovitz number} (the wrinkled, corrugated and thin-reaction-zone regimes, $\mathrm{Ka}\lesssim 100$), with reasonably fast, near-manifold chemistry~\cite{lecture10,peters2000turbulent,veynante2002turbulent}. There the thermochemistry collapses onto a low-dimensional manifold parametrised by $Z$ and/or $c$ (and $\chi$), so flamelet/FGM tabulation with a presumed PDF is \textbf{accurate and very cheap} --- the natural choice for design-oriented RANS/LES of most premixed and non-premixed devices (e.g.\ Sandia flame D). \textbf{Transported-PDF methods are recommended when chemistry and turbulence cannot be decoupled} --- \textbf{low Da / high Ka} (broken/distributed reaction zones, well-stirred-reactor regime), and whenever \textbf{finite-rate effects dominate}: strong \textbf{local extinction and re-ignition} (Sandia flames E and F), \textbf{slow chemistry} such as \ce{NOx} and CO that lags the main heat release, and strongly unsteady or non-manifold behaviour~\cite{lecture10,pope2000turbulent,veynante2002turbulent}. Their advantage is the \emph{exact} treatment of the reaction rate with no flame-structure assumption; their cost (and the modelled micromixing term) is the price paid, so they are reserved for the regimes and quantities where flamelet models are known to fail. In short: \textbf{flamelet where a manifold captures the state, transported PDF where it does not}.
