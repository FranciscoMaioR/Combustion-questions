\subsection{Non-Premixed Combustion}

\subsubsection{What is the typical structure of a laminar diffusion flame?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9 (Laminar and Turbulent Flames); Maas notes Ch.13.1, 13.3, 13.4 / Warnatz Ch.13 (Nonpremixed Flames)]}

\paragraph{Answer}
Oxidizer on one side, fuel on the other. The flame is a thin layer balancing the transport of both reactants and products.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
In a laminar diffusion (non-premixed) flame the fuel and oxidiser are supplied in \textbf{separate} streams and must \textbf{interdiffuse} before they can react, so the whole process is controlled by molecular mixing rather than by chemical propagation~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion,glassman2014combustion}. The reaction is confined to a \textbf{thin reaction sheet} located where fuel and oxidiser meet in stoichiometric proportion, i.e.\ at the iso-surface of stoichiometric \textbf{mixture fraction} $Z=Z_{st}$. Fuel diffuses from the fuel side and oxygen from the oxidiser side towards this sheet; heat and products generated there diffuse \emph{outward} in both directions. Consequently the \textbf{peak temperature occurs at $Z_{st}$}, and the structure plotted against the spatial coordinate is a single-peaked temperature profile with fuel present only on the rich side and oxidiser only on the lean side~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical}. In the limit of infinitely fast (one-step) chemistry the structure is the \textbf{Burke--Schumann} solution: fuel and oxidiser cannot coexist, and $Y_F$, $Y_{O_2}$ and $T$ become piecewise-linear functions of $Z$ with their kink/peak at $Z_{st}$; finite-rate chemistry rounds these profiles and lets some reactant leak through the sheet~\cite{poinsot2012theoretical,lecture9}. Because the burning rate is set by the rate at which the streams are mixed, the controlling parameter is the local strain / \textbf{scalar dissipation rate} rather than a flame speed (see the following questions). Everyday examples are the candle flame and the laminar gas-jet (e.g.\ Wolfhard--Parker) flame.
}




\subsubsection{Why is there no such thing as a laminar flame speed of a diffusion flame?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 9; Maas notes Ch.13 / Warnatz Ch.13 (Nonpremixed Flames)]}

\paragraph{Answer}

The laminar flame speed is not dependent on the reactants, only on the mixing rate; these flames are limited by the mixing rate, not by the reaction rate.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
A laminar flame speed is a property that exists \emph{only} for \textbf{premixed} flames. It is the eigenvalue speed at which a self-sustaining reaction--diffusion wave propagates into a \textbf{homogeneous combustible mixture} lying ahead of it, $s_L^0\propto\sqrt{\alpha/\tau_{chem}}$~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. A diffusion flame has \textbf{no such premixed mixture ahead of the front}: fuel and oxidiser are stored separately and are not combustible until they have mixed, so there is no medium for a propagating wave to travel through, and hence no characteristic propagation velocity to define~\cite{lecture9,glassman2014combustion}. Instead, the flame simply \textbf{anchors itself where the reactants arrive in stoichiometric proportion} ($Z=Z_{st}$); its position and intensity are dictated by how fast convection and diffusion bring fuel and oxidiser together, i.e.\ by the \textbf{mixing rate}, quantified by the strain rate / \textbf{scalar dissipation rate} $\chi$ (and the associated Damk\"ohler number), not by a burning velocity~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical,warnatz2006combustion}. This is exactly why such flames are called \emph{mixing-controlled}: increasing the mixing (strain) raises the local burning rate up to the point of extinction, whereas a premixed flame responds by changing its propagation speed --- an option the diffusion flame does not have.
}

\textbf{Definition.} The mixture fraction $Z$ is the mass fraction of material at a point that \textbf{originated in the fuel stream}, $Z = m_1/(m_1+m_2)$, so $Z=1$ in the fuel inlet and $Z=0$ in the oxidiser inlet, and $Z$ varies between these on any line crossing the flame~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion}. For \textbf{impure streams} (fuel diluted with inert, air rather than pure \ce{O2}), $Z$ is built from a conserved combination of species. Writing one-step chemistry $\nu_F' F + \nu_{O}' O \to P$, the stoichiometric oxidiser-to-fuel mass ratio is $s = (Y_O^0/Y_F^0)_{st} = \nu_O' W_O/(\nu_F' W_F)$ (e.g.\ $s=4$ for \ce{CH4}); since $dY_{O_2} = s\,dY_F$, the combination $sY_F - Y_{O_2}$ has no chemical source, and normalising with the inlet values $Y_F^0$ (fuel in stream 1) and $Y_O^0$ (oxidiser in stream 2) gives the useful definition~\cite{lecture9}
\begin{equation*}
Z = \frac{sY_F - Y_{O_2} + Y_{O_2}^0}{sY_F^0 + Y_{O_2}^0}.
\end{equation*}
The \textbf{stoichiometric value} (where complete consumption is possible, i.e.\ the flame location) is $Z_{st} = 1/\!\left(1 + sY_F^0/Y_O^0\right)$, and it relates to the equivalence ratio by $\phi = \tfrac{Z}{1-Z}\tfrac{1-Z_{st}}{Z_{st}}$~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical}. In practice the species-based definition is hard to measure, so \textbf{Bilger's} element-based form is used: because chemical reactions conserve \emph{elements}, the element mass fractions ($Z_C,Z_H,Z_O$) give a reaction-independent $Z$, $Z = \big[Z_C/(mW_C)+Z_H/(nW_H)+2(Y_{O_2,2}-Z_O)/(\nu_{O_2}'W_{O_2})\big]/[\dots]_1$~\cite{lecture9,bilger1989structure}. This also answers how \textbf{products} are handled: $Z$ counts only the \emph{origin} (fuel- vs oxidiser-stream atoms) and is blind to molecular form, so product species automatically carry the mixture fraction of the local gas and require no separate treatment.

\textbf{Conditions for $Z$ to be a useful variable.} (i) a \textbf{two-feed} configuration (one fuel stream, one oxidiser stream) with well-defined, uniform inlet conditions; (ii) \textbf{equal diffusivities} of all species (unity Lewis numbers), so that every conserved scalar obeys the \emph{same} convection--diffusion equation and $Z$ is uniquely defined --- differential diffusion (hydrogen) is the main thing that spoils this; (iii) for the flamelet description, a \textbf{thin reaction zone} (fast chemistry) so that the state is well parametrised by $Z$~\cite{lecture9,warnatz2006combustion,peters2000turbulent}.

\textbf{Splitting into a mixing problem and a chemistry problem.} The power of $Z$ is that it is a \textbf{conserved scalar}: it satisfies a transport equation with \textbf{no chemical source term},
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial(\rho Z)}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial(\rho Z v_j)}{\partial x_j} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\!\left(\rho D_Z\frac{\partial Z}{\partial x_j}\right).
\end{equation*}
Hence the problem decouples: (1) a pure \textbf{mixing problem} --- solve the (turbulent) transport of the passive scalar $Z$ to find where fuel-origin and oxidiser-origin mass go; and (2) a separate \textbf{chemistry problem} --- determine the thermochemical state as a \emph{state relationship} $Y_k = Y_k(Z)$ and $T=T(Z)$ (from equilibrium, Burke--Schumann, or a flamelet calculation). The flame structure is then reconstructed by composing the two, $Y_k(\vec x) = Y_k(Z(\vec x))$. In the infinitely-fast-chemistry (\textbf{Burke--Schumann}) limit these state relationships are the piecewise-linear profiles of Fig.~\ref{fig:nonpremixed-irreversible-limit} (irreversible) and the slightly rounded equilibrium curves of Fig.~\ref{fig:nonpremixed-reversible-limit} (reversible), both peaking at $Z_{st}$; finite-rate effects (parametrised by the scalar dissipation rate) move the state off these equilibrium lines, as discussed in the flamelet questions below~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical}.
}

\subsubsection{Define mixture fraction. In which circumstances can enthalpy be considered equivalent to mixture fraction?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Maas notes Ch.13 / Warnatz Ch.14 (Turbulent Non-Premixed Flames); Lecture 9/10]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Enthalpy can be related to Z in well-posed BC and adiabatic systems.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
Both the (total) \textbf{enthalpy} $h$ and the \textbf{mixture fraction} $Z$ are \textbf{conserved scalars}: $Z$ has no chemical source by construction, and $h$ has none because combustion only converts chemical into sensible enthalpy, leaving the total unchanged~\cite{lecture8,warnatz2006combustion}. When the two satisfy the \emph{same} convection--diffusion equation and have linearly-related boundary values, two such scalars are linearly related \emph{everywhere} in the field. Therefore enthalpy can be considered \textbf{equivalent to (a linear function of) mixture fraction},
\begin{equation*}
h(\vec x) = h_{ox} + Z(\vec x)\,(h_{fuel} - h_{ox}),
\end{equation*}
under the following circumstances~\cite{lecture9,peters2000turbulent,poinsot2012theoretical}: (i) the system is \textbf{adiabatic} --- no heat loss and negligible radiation, so there is no enthalpy sink; (ii) \textbf{low-Mach / steady, isobaric} flow, so pressure-work and kinetic-energy terms are negligible; (iii) \textbf{equal diffusivities / unity Lewis number}, so $h$ and $Z$ diffuse \emph{identically} (otherwise differential diffusion decouples them); and (iv) each inlet stream has a \textbf{uniform enthalpy} (well-posed, constant boundary conditions). When these hold, $h$ carries no independent information and the thermochemistry can be tabulated against $Z$ alone. Conversely, \textbf{heat loss, radiation, or non-unity Lewis numbers} break the equivalence, and then enthalpy must be retained as an \textbf{additional independent coordinate} (an extra manifold dimension, e.g.\ in non-adiabatic FGM)~\cite{warnatz2006combustion,lecture10}.
}

\paragraph{Claude answer}
This is \emph{not} the same as the general averaging/transported-PDF discussion: here the question is specifically how to obtain \textbf{mean} quantities in a turbulent non-premixed flame once the thermochemistry is already known as a state relationship $\phi = \phi(Z)$ (or $\phi(Z,\chi)$) from the flamelet/conserved-scalar picture~\cite{lecture10,warnatz2006combustion}. Because $Z$ fluctuates turbulently, the Favre-mean of any quantity is $\tilde\phi(\vec x) = \int_0^1 \phi(Z)\,\tilde P(Z;\vec x)\,dZ$, so we need the \textbf{PDF of the mixture fraction}, $\tilde P(Z)$. Rather than solving a transport equation for the PDF (the expensive transported-PDF route), the \textbf{presumed-PDF (assumed-shape) approach} postulates a functional form for $\tilde P(Z)$ that is fixed by just its first two moments --- the Favre mean $\tilde Z$ and the variance $\widetilde{Z''^2}$~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,peters2000turbulent}. The standard choice is the \textbf{$\beta$-function PDF}, $\tilde P(Z;\tilde Z,\widetilde{Z''^2})$, which with only two parameters can represent the full range of mixing states: a narrow spike at $\tilde Z$ when the variance is small (well mixed), and a double-peaked, nearly bimodal shape approaching $\alpha\,\delta(Z)+\beta\,\delta(1-Z)$ when the variance is large (unmixed fuel and oxidiser pockets). Pre-integrating the flamelet/equilibrium library over this PDF gives the mean reactive scalars and reaction rates directly, e.g.\ $\tilde Y_k = \int_0^1 Y_k(Z)\,\tilde P(Z;\tilde Z,\widetilde{Z''^2})\,dZ$ (extended to $\iint \cdots \tilde P(Z,\chi)\,dZ\,d\chi$ when scalar dissipation is retained). The cost is two extra transport equations --- for $\tilde Z$ and $\widetilde{Z''^2}$ (the latter introduced in the next question) --- and the assumption that the chosen shape is adequate and that the state is a function of $Z$ (flamelet hypothesis). It is favoured precisely because it is far cheaper than transported PDF methods while still accounting for turbulent fluctuations~\cite{lecture10,peters2000turbulent}.
}






\subsubsection{Explain why one is interested in calculating the variance of mixture fraction. How does the scalar dissipation rate influence the variance of mixture fraction?}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.14 (mixture fraction variance, scalar dissipation rate); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer}

Mixture fraction variation is used to find the point where the flame is, at the point of stoichiometric conditions.

When the change of coordinates is introduced, to Z and its normal counterparts, the equations are rewritten and the flamelet equations are obtained. These equations need the concentration of species as a function of Z.

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
\textbf{Why the variance is needed.} The presumed-PDF closure of the previous question requires \emph{two} parameters; the \textbf{mixture-fraction variance} $\widetilde{Z''^2}$ is the second one, and it sets the \emph{width} of the assumed $\beta$-PDF. Physically it measures the intensity of the turbulent fluctuations of $Z$ about its mean --- i.e.\ how far the fluid at a point is from being fully mixed: $\widetilde{Z''^2}\to0$ means perfectly mixed (PDF collapses to a spike, $\tilde\phi = \phi(\tilde Z)$), while a large variance means unmixed fuel/oxidiser still coexist, which drastically changes the mean reaction rate~\cite{lecture10,veynante2002turbulent,peters2000turbulent}. Without it the unmixedness --- and hence the turbulence--chemistry interaction --- cannot be captured.

\textbf{Role of the scalar dissipation rate.} The variance obeys its own transport equation in which it is \textbf{produced} by the mean scalar gradient (turbulent flux acting on $\nabla\tilde Z$) and \textbf{destroyed} by molecular mixing, the sink being the \textbf{scalar dissipation rate}
\begin{equation*}
\tilde\chi = 2\,\overline{D\,|\nabla Z''|^2} \;\; \big(\text{instantaneously } \chi = 2D\,\nabla Z\cdot\nabla Z\big).
\end{equation*}
Thus $\chi$ is precisely the rate at which mixture-fraction fluctuations are smoothed out: a high scalar dissipation rate rapidly destroys variance, narrowing the PDF and driving the fluid towards the mixed state, whereas low $\chi$ lets fluctuations (and unmixedness) persist~\cite{lecture10,warnatz2006combustion,peters2000turbulent}. In practice the variance budget is often closed algebraically by assuming production~$\approx$~dissipation, giving a \textbf{linear-relaxation (mixing-frequency) model} $\tilde\chi \approx C_\chi\,(\varepsilon/k)\,\widetilde{Z''^2}$, i.e.\ $\widetilde{Z''^2}$ and $\tilde\chi$ are directly proportional through the turbulent mixing frequency $\varepsilon/k$ (or, in LES, a subgrid analogue). The same $\chi$ is also the parameter that controls departure from equilibrium and extinction in the flamelet equations (next question), so the variance budget and the flamelet structure are coupled through the single quantity $\chi$~\cite{lecture10,peters2000turbulent}.
}




\subsubsection{Explain the steady laminar flamelet model of turbulent non-premixed combustion.}

{\color{gray}\footnotesize [Claude context: check Lecture 10; Warnatz Ch.14 (flamelet model for non-premixed combustion); combustion\_modeling.pdf (supplementary, verify against Warnatz)]}

\paragraph{Answer}
The flamelet assumption is that mixing does not happen in the flame, and that the chemical time scales are much faster than the mixing time scales.
... lecture 9

{\color{istblue}
\paragraph{Claude answer}
The steady laminar flamelet model regards a turbulent non-premixed flame as a statistical ensemble of thin, locally one-dimensional \textbf{laminar diffusion flamelets} that are embedded in, convected by and strained by the surrounding turbulence~\cite{lecture9,peters2000turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}. It rests on the \textbf{flamelet assumption}: the reaction zone is thin compared with all turbulent scales (high Damk\"ohler number / thin reaction zone), so turbulence wrinkles and strains the sheet but does not penetrate its internal structure --- confirming and sharpening the draft, the chemical time scale is much shorter than the mixing time scale, so reaction stays confined to a thin layer around $Z_{st}$. One then changes independent variables from physical coordinates to the mixture fraction $Z$ (normal to the front) plus tangential coordinates; \textbf{neglecting transport parallel to the front} reduces the species and temperature balances to the \textbf{steady flamelet equations}~\cite{lecture9,peters2000turbulent}
\begin{equation*}
0 = \rho\,\frac{\chi}{2}\,\frac{\partial^2 Y_k}{\partial Z^2} + \dot\omega_k, \qquad 0 = \rho\,\frac{\chi}{2}\,\frac{\partial^2 T}{\partial Z^2} + \dot\omega_T,
\end{equation*}
in which the \textbf{scalar dissipation rate} $\chi = 2D\,|\nabla Z|^2$ is the \emph{only} quantity coupling the flamelet to the flow field: it represents the diffusion/strain imposed by the (turbulent) mixing and replaces the spatial transport terms. The structure thus expresses the steady-state \textbf{balance between diffusion and chemistry}. Solving the equations for a prescribed $\chi(Z)$ --- often taken from the counterflow profile $\chi = (a/\pi)\exp\!\big(-2[\mathrm{erf}^{-1}(1-2Z)]^2\big)$, parametrised by its stoichiometric value $\chi_{st}\equiv\chi(Z_{st})$ --- yields state relationships $Y_k = Y_k(Z,\chi_{st})$ and $T = T(Z,\chi_{st})$~\cite{lecture9,poinsot2012theoretical}. \textbf{Limiting behaviour:} as $\chi_{st}\to 0$ the solution approaches chemical \textbf{equilibrium} (Burke--Schumann); increasing $\chi_{st}$ pulls the state progressively off equilibrium (lower peak temperature, reactant leakage); and beyond a critical \textbf{quenching} value $\chi_{st,q}$ the flamelet \textbf{extinguishes}, leaving pure mixing without combustion~\cite{lecture9,peters2000turbulent}. In a turbulent computation these laminar flamelet libraries are tabulated and combined with a presumed joint PDF $\tilde P(Z,\chi)$ to deliver Favre-mean fields. \textbf{Advantages:} the expensive detailed chemistry is pre-computed once and decoupled from the flow, making it very efficient. \textbf{Drawbacks:} it assumes fast chemistry and a thin, quasi-steady flamelet, so it struggles with strongly unsteady effects, slow chemistry (e.g.\ \ce{NOx}, CO, ignition/extinction transients) and lift-off, for which \textbf{unsteady} flamelets or extra parameters/manifold dimensions are required~\cite{peters2000turbulent,warnatz2006combustion}.
}
